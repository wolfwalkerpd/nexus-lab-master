If you run a dental practice and you're collecting quotes, you've probably noticed the prices are all over the place. One agency says £500, the next says £8,000, and neither really explains why. So how much should a dental practice pay for a website? The honest answer is that it depends on what you need the site to do — not on how impressive the invoice looks.

This is a plain-English guide to real UK price ranges, what actually drives the cost, and how to avoid paying for things you don't need. No sales spin — just the numbers we'd give a friend.

## The short answer: what UK practices actually pay

For a single-location dental practice in the UK, most sensible options fall into three broad bands. A DIY builder or an off-the-shelf template costs you £0 to around £500, but you do the work and it usually shows. A freelancer or small studio typically charges £1,000 to £3,000 for a proper, custom-built site. A larger agency doing fully bespoke design, with booking systems and ongoing marketing baked in, can run from £5,000 to £15,000 or more.

Most independent practices don't need the top band. A well-built site in the £1,000 to £3,000 range does everything a busy local practice needs: it looks professional, loads fast on a phone, and turns visitors into booked appointments.

## What pushes the price up

The gap between a £500 site and a £5,000 one usually comes down to a handful of things. Some are worth paying for, some aren't — it depends on your practice.

- Custom design versus a template — a layout built around your practice costs more than a stock theme, but it won't look like every other dentist in town.
- The number of pages — a five-page site is quicker to build than a 20-page one with a page for every treatment.
- Copywriting — words that persuade a nervous patient to book take time to write well.
- Photography — professional photos of your practice and team beat stock images, but they add to the bill.
- Online booking and integrations — connecting to your practice management or booking software adds development time.
- SEO and accessibility — proper local SEO and GDPR-friendly handling of patient enquiries are worth building in from the start.

## Why the cheapest option often costs more

A £300 site feels like a bargain until you count the hidden costs. Cheap builds are often slow, hard to update, and tied to a platform you can't leave without starting again. Plenty of practices end up paying twice: once for the cheap site, then again a year later for the rebuild.

Whatever you spend, make sure you own what you're paying for. If an agency keeps your domain, your content or your data, you don't really own your website — you're renting it. Dentistry is a competitive [industry](/industries), and a site you can't control is a site you can't grow.

> The cheapest website is rarely the one that costs the least.
