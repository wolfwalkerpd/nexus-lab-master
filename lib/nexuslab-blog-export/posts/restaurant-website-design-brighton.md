Brighton eats out, and it decides fast. From the brunch queues in the North Laine to the late tables on Preston Street, this is a city that picks where to spend its money in seconds — usually on a phone, and often while walking somewhere else. Strong restaurant website design in Brighton is not about looking pretty on a big monitor back in a studio. It is about winning that quick, hungry decision on a phone screen, in the rain, outside the Pavilion.

Whether you run a small cafe in Kemptown, a seafood place near the seafront or a vegan kitchen off Trafalgar Street, your website has one job: turn a search into someone sitting at your table. Here is what that takes in a city this busy, this walkable and this particular about where it eats.

## Brighton diners search on their phones, mid-walk

Most local restaurant searches in Brighton happen on mobile, and plenty of them happen on the move. Picture the day-trippers stepping off the London train at Brighton station wanting lunch within ten minutes' walk, the crowds drifting along the seafront the moment the sun breaks through, or students from the University of Sussex and the University of Brighton hunting for somewhere cheap and open late. They type 'brunch near me' or 'vegan restaurant Brighton', glance at the first few results and pick one. If your site loads slowly or pinches awkwardly on a phone, you have lost them before they have read a word.

This is where [local SEO](/services/seo) earns its keep. Being found for the searches Brighton actually makes — by neighbourhood, by dish, by 'open now' near The Lanes or Seven Dials — matters as much as the design itself. A beautiful website that nobody finds feeds nobody.

## Your menu is the page everyone opens first

Ask almost any restaurant what their most-visited page is and it is the menu, every time. Yet so many Brighton sites still bury it inside a slow PDF that forces people to zoom, drag and squint. On a phone, that friction is enough to send someone straight to the place next door.

Your menu should be a proper web page: quick to load, easy to read and simple to scan with a thumb. In a city as veggie- and vegan-friendly as Brighton, clear dietary labelling is not a nice-to-have — for a lot of your customers it is the whole decision.

- **Live text, not a PDF**, so it loads instantly and shows up in Google
- Clear vegan, vegetarian, gluten-free and allergen labels
- Honest prices, kept up to date, so there are no awkward surprises at the table
- A few photos of your real signature dishes, not stock images of food you don't serve
- Sections people can jump straight to — brunch, small plates, puddings, drinks — without endless scrolling

## Make booking a table effortless

Once someone likes the look of your menu, do not make them work to sit down. A booking button that follows them down the page, a phone number that dials with one tap, and opening hours you can read at a glance will do more for your covers than any amount of clever design. If you welcome walk-ins, say so plainly. If you get slammed on sunny Saturdays, let people book ahead so you are not turning them away at the door.

Brighton trade lives and dies by the weather and the calendar. The first warm weekend fills the seafront, and Fringe, the Festival and Pride pack the whole city. Your website should make it easy to grab a table on those days and to spot your offers — a set lunch, a pre-theatre menu before a show at the Theatre Royal, a quiet-Tuesday deal — without hunting three clicks deep.

> The best restaurant website is the one that gets someone through your door before they have finished reading it.

## Reviews and your Google listing do half the selling

Before anyone reaches your website, many will see your Google Business Profile first — that little panel with your star rating, photos and opening hours. In a food city as crowded as Brighton, those stars and a handful of recent photos often decide whether someone clicks through at all. Keep the profile current, reply to your reviews, and make sure the photos look like a real Tuesday night in your place rather than a stock library.

Your website should back all of that up. Show genuine reviews, point people to where they can leave one, and keep everything accurate — hours, festival opening times over Fringe and Pride, and the menu itself. A tidy [website design](/services/website-design) that matches what people already saw on Google builds the trust that turns a browser into a booking, and keeping it all current is exactly what a [care plan](/services/care-plans) is for.
