If you run a shop, cafe, clinic or trade business, website accessibility law in the UK can feel like something only big corporations need to worry about. It isn't. The same duties that apply to your physical premises reach your website too, and the rules are tightening, not loosening.

This is what you must know, in plain English: which laws actually apply to a small-business website, whether you're at risk, and the handful of fixes that make the biggest difference. No scare tactics, no legalese.

## What website accessibility law in the UK actually says

There isn't a single 'website law' in this country. A few different rules overlap, and it helps to know which is which.

Running through all of it is one technical benchmark: the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, or WCAG. The current version to aim for is WCAG 2.2 at level AA. It isn't a law in itself, but it's the yardstick courts, regulators and auditors reach for. It boils down to four ideas: your content should be perceivable, operable, understandable and robust.

- The **Equality Act 2010** is the big one. It requires businesses to make 'reasonable adjustments' so disabled people aren't put at a substantial disadvantage, and that includes your website, not just your front door.
- The **Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018** apply to councils, the NHS and other public bodies. You're probably not covered, but they set the standard everyone now measures against: WCAG.
- The **European Accessibility Act** came into force in June 2025. If you sell products or services to customers in the EU, such as an online shop or booking system, it may apply to you even from the UK.

## Does it apply to your small business?

For most UK small businesses the honest answer is yes, through the Equality Act. If members of the public can buy from you, book with you or contact you online, you have a duty to make that reasonably possible for disabled visitors. There's no accessibility inspector doing the rounds, but the duty is real, and complaints can turn into legal claims.

The European Accessibility Act adds a second layer for anyone trading into the EU. Very small businesses may be exempt from parts of it, but the exemptions are narrower than most people hope. If you'd rather not untangle which rule bites first, the safe move is simply to build to WCAG 2.2 AA and be done with it.

## The fixes that make the biggest difference

You don't need to become an accessibility expert. A large share of real-world problems trace back to a short list of basics that any decent build should get right in the first place.

Get those right and you've cleared most of the bar. Nearly all of them overlap with good [website design](/services/website-design) anyway, so a site built properly from day one rarely needs an expensive retrofit later.

- **Colour contrast:** text needs to stand out clearly from its background. Pale grey on white might look elegant, but plenty of people simply can't read it.
- **Keyboard navigation:** every link, button and form should work without a mouse. Test it by pressing Tab through your own page.
- **Alt text on images:** short descriptions that let screen readers, and search engines, understand what a picture shows.
- **Labelled forms:** each field needs a clear, programmatic label, not just placeholder text that vanishes the moment you start typing.
- **Readable structure:** proper headings, sensible link text rather than 'click here', and captions on any video.

Here's the part that gets missed. Accessibility and search rankings pull in the same direction. Build for them and you build for everyone who lands on your site.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is my small business website legally required to be accessible?

In most cases, yes. The Equality Act 2010 requires businesses serving the public to make reasonable adjustments so disabled people aren't put at a disadvantage, and that covers your website. There's no routine inspection, but the duty is real and complaints can become legal claims. Building to WCAG 2.2 AA is the practical way to meet it.

### What is WCAG and which version should I follow?

WCAG stands for the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, the internationally recognised standard for accessible websites. Aim for the current version, WCAG 2.2, at level AA. It covers things like colour contrast, keyboard access and clear structure, and it's the benchmark regulators and courts refer to.
