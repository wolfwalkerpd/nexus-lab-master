If you run a small business, you probably don't think about website security until something breaks. But learning how to keep your website secure with SSL, backups and updates is far easier than it sounds — and it protects the one thing that brings you enquiries. You don't need to be technical. You need three habits and a bit of routine.

This guide walks through the three that matter most for a local business site: the padlock that tells visitors they're safe, the safety net that gets you back online after a mistake, and the updates that quietly close the doors attackers walk through. We'll keep it plain, and we'll be honest at the end about when it's worth handing the whole lot to someone else.

## Start with SSL: the padlock in the address bar

SSL is the technology behind the little padlock you see in your browser's address bar. When a site has it, the web address starts with **https** instead of http, and everything sent between your visitor and your site — a contact form, a booking, an enquiry — is encrypted. Without it, modern browsers show a blunt 'Not secure' warning right where people decide whether to trust you.

For a local business that mostly means one thing: lost enquiries. Someone searching for a plumber in Leeds or a dentist in Bristol isn't going to hand their phone number to a site the browser is warning them about. The good news is that SSL is usually free through your hosting, and a proper [website build](/services/website-design) should have it switched on and forcing https from day one.

It matters for Google too. HTTPS is a small but real ranking signal, so it quietly supports your [local SEO](/services/seo) alongside everything else you do. If your padlock is missing, or your certificate has quietly expired, that's the first thing to put right.

## Back up your site before you ever need to

A backup is a saved copy of your website you can restore if something goes wrong — and eventually something does. A plugin update breaks a page, a login gets compromised, or someone on your team deletes the wrong thing. With a recent backup, that's a ten-minute fix. Without one, it can mean rebuilding your site from scratch.

The most common problem we see in a teardown isn't the absence of backups — it's a business that assumes the host is handling it and has never actually checked. Five minutes confirming where your backups live, and how you'd restore one, is time very well spent.

- Back up the whole site — files and database — not just one or the other.
- Keep backups automatic and daily, or at least weekly, so nothing depends on you remembering.
- Store copies somewhere separate from your hosting, so one server problem can't take down both the site and its backup.
- Test a restore once, so you know it works before a real emergency, not during one.
- Keep a few weeks of history rather than only last night's copy, in case a problem goes unnoticed for a while.

## Keep everything updated — this is where most sites get caught

Most small-business sites run on software: a content system such as WordPress, a theme, and a handful of plugins. Each of those gets updates, and a lot of those updates exist to close security holes that are already public knowledge. Skip them for months and you're leaving known, published doors unlocked — which is exactly what automated attacks go looking for.

Updating is mostly straightforward, but it isn't risk-free: now and then an update clashes with something and breaks a page, which is precisely why the backup habit comes first. Work in a sensible order — back up, update, then check the site still works, paying special attention to your contact form and any booking or payment steps.

## How to keep your website secure without becoming an expert

You don't need to do any of this daily. A short monthly routine covers most small-business sites and takes less time than a coffee break. Check three things: that your padlock is still there and your SSL certificate hasn't lapsed, that a recent backup exists and you know how to restore it, and that your core software, theme and plugins are up to date. Click through your key pages afterwards and you've handled the vast majority of what actually goes wrong.

Two extra habits make a real difference for very little effort. Use strong, unique passwords with two-factor login on your admin account, and remove any plugins or user accounts you no longer use. Every unused thing sitting on your site is one more thing that can be exploited.

> Security for a small business isn't a project — it's a fifteen-minute habit you repeat once a month.

### Do I really need SSL if I only have a simple brochure website?

Yes. Even without a shop or logins, browsers now label any http site as 'Not secure', and that warning sits right next to your business name at the exact moment a potential customer is deciding whether to trust you. SSL is usually free through your hosting, so there's no good reason to go without it.

### How often should I back up my website?

For most small-business sites, a daily or at least weekly automatic backup, stored separately from your hosting, is plenty. The key is that it's automatic, off-site and tested — a backup you've never tried to restore is really just a hopeful guess.
