## What Newcastle diners do before they visit

Newcastle eats out a lot, and it decides fast. Most of that deciding happens on a phone, often minutes before people set off. Someone typing 'restaurants near me' on the Quayside, a group sorting food before kick-off at St James' Park, a couple in Jesmond scrolling brunch spots on a Saturday morning — they all do the same three things. They check your menu, they look at your photos, and they see whether they can book. If any of those is missing or awkward, they move on, and there is rarely a second visit to a website that frustrated them the first time.

The city's independent scene makes this sharper. ## The features that actually fill tables

A good hospitality website is not complicated. It just needs to do a handful of jobs really well:

- An up-to-date menu that loads as fast, readable text — not a tiny PDF people have to pinch and zoom to read on a phone.
- Online booking, or a simple table-request form, that works in a thumb-tap so you're not tied to the phone mid-service.
- Your offers and events front and centre — bottomless brunch, match-day deals, the Sunday roast that always sells out.
- Real photos of your food and your room. Newcastle diners can spot a stock image from a mile off.
- Reviews and social proof placed where people actually look, not buried three clicks deep.
- Click-to-call and directions, because plenty of visitors only want your number and where to park.

> The best restaurant website answers 'what's on the menu and can I get a table?' before anyone has to ask.

## Built for the phone first, not the laptop

Picture your customer's real situation. They're walking down Grey Street in the rain, one hand on their phone, deciding in under a minute. If your menu opens as a slow PDF, or your booking form needs pinching and zooming, you've already lost them. Mobile-first means the phone version is the real version — big tap targets, fast loading, your number and address one tap away. Everything else is built out from there. A [website built around bookings and menus](/services/website-design) should feel effortless on a five-inch screen first, and simply look after itself on a laptop second.

## Getting found on Google in Newcastle

A beautiful site nobody finds won't fill your tables. When someone searches 'best coffee in Heaton' or 'Sunday lunch near me', two things decide whether you show up: your Google Business Profile and the local search wording on your own site. Keep your profile complete — opening hours, current photos, the right category — and reply to your reviews. On the site, mention the neighbourhoods you serve, keep your address identical everywhere it appears, and make sure each page is genuinely about what you offer. It's steady, unglamorous work, but it's how a small place in Byker or Gosforth ends up above the chains.
