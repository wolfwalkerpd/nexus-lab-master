When someone finds your restaurant online, they are usually hungry, in a hurry and deciding fast. Get the restaurant website essentials right — menus, bookings and more — and you turn that quick look into a booked table. Get them wrong and the diner taps back to Google and picks the place down the road.

You do not need a flashy site with clever animations to win that decision. You need the handful of things every diner is actually looking for, laid out so clearly that booking feels like the obvious next step. Here is what matters, and why.

## Start with the menu — it's what they came for

The menu is the number one reason people visit a restaurant website. Not your founding story, not your interior shots — the food and the prices. So make it impossible to miss and easy to read.

Put the menu one tap from your homepage and show it as real text on the page, not a PDF or a photo of a printed sheet. PDFs are slow to load, awkward to read on a phone and invisible to Google. If a visitor has to pinch and zoom to read your starters, you have already spent a little of their goodwill. Real text also means a search engine can read your dishes, which helps you show up when someone nearby searches for something like wood-fired pizza or a Sunday roast.

Keep your prices current, too. Nothing sours a first visit like arriving to find the £12 main is now £16. If your menu changes often, this is exactly the kind of thing a well-built [restaurant website](/services/website-design) with a simple admin panel should let you edit yourself in minutes.

## Make booking a table effortless

A diner who is ready to book is a diner you do not want to make work. Aim for the fewest possible steps between this looks good and table booked.

If you take online bookings, put a clear booking option on every page — in the header, next to the menu, and again at the foot. If you take bookings by phone, make the number tappable so a mobile visitor can call in one touch rather than copying digits across apps. Whatever you use, avoid dumping people onto a clunky third-party page that looks nothing like your brand and demands ten fields before it will hold a table.

For quieter weeknights, pairing that booking button with a line like walk-ins always welcome reassures the spontaneous crowd as well as the planners.

> Every extra click or form field between hungry and booked is a chance for the diner to change their mind.

## Win on mobile, and win on speed

Most people looking for somewhere to eat are doing it on a phone — often stood on the pavement or sat in the car, deciding then and there. If your site is slow or fiddly on mobile, they will not wait for it.

That means large, tappable buttons, text you can read without zooming, and pages that load in a couple of seconds. Heavy background videos and enormous, uncompressed photos are the usual culprits behind a sluggish restaurant site. Trim the image sizes, keep the design clean, and test the whole thing on a real phone rather than only your laptop.

## Help hungry locals find you

Being found is half the battle, and for restaurants that battle is almost entirely local. When someone types Sunday roast near me or best curry in the town centre, Google leans heavily on your Google Business Profile and the signals coming from your website.

Claim and fully fill out your Google Business Profile, keep your name, address and phone number identical everywhere they appear, and make sure your opening hours are right — especially over bank holidays and Christmas. Strong [local SEO](/services/seo) ties your website and your profile together, so you turn up in the map results where hungry locals are actually looking.

## The trust details that tip a browser into a booking

Once a visitor likes the look of your food, a few practical details decide whether they commit. Leave them out and even a keen diner hesitates.

Make sure your website clearly covers:

None of this needs to be clever. It just needs to be there, accurate and quick to find.

- Opening hours, including kitchen last-orders and any different weekend times
- Your full address with a map link, plus a note on parking or the nearest station
- A few honest, well-lit photos of the food, the room and the people who run it
- Dietary information — vegan, vegetarian, gluten-free and clear allergen notes
- Whether you are dog-friendly, child-friendly or set up for larger groups
- A link to your live reviews so newcomers can see you are trusted

- Lead with the menu as readable text, not a PDF, and keep prices up to date.
- Make booking a one- or two-tap action, with a tappable phone number on mobile.
