You paid for a website, but do you actually know if it's working? For a lot of UK small-business owners, the site goes live and then just sits there, a bit of a black box. Learning how to track your website's performance is analytics 101, and it's the difference between guessing and knowing. Once you can see what visitors actually do, you can fix what's costing you enquiries and put more into what's already working.

The good news is you don't need to be a data analyst or buy expensive software. A handful of free tools and a few numbers that genuinely matter will tell you most of what you need to know. Here's how to set it up, what to look at, and how to turn it into more phone calls and bookings.

## Start with the free tools that do the heavy lifting

You can track almost everything that matters with two free tools from Google, plus whatever comes built into your site. Between them they answer two different questions: how people find you, and what they do once they arrive.

- **Google Analytics 4 (GA4)** — the free standard for seeing how many people visit, which pages they land on, how long they stay and where they leave. It works on any website, big or small.
- **Google Search Console** — shows which search terms bring people to you, roughly where you rank for each, and any technical issues Google has flagged. It's the backbone of good [SEO](/services/seo).
- **Google Business Profile insights** — for local firms, this reveals how many people found you on Maps, called, or asked for directions. Often your biggest source of nearby customers.
- **Your form and phone log** — the numbers that pay the bills. Jot down where new enquiries heard about you, so you can tie website visits back to real work won.

## How to track your website's performance: analytics 101

At its heart, analytics is a simple loop: measure what's happening now, change one thing, then check whether the number moved. You don't need to understand every chart on the screen. Set a baseline, pick one goal — usually more enquiries — and follow the handful of metrics that feed it.

The single most useful thing you can do is set up conversion tracking. Tell Google Analytics to record when someone submits your contact form, taps your phone number or reaches a 'thank you' page. Now, instead of vanity figures like total visits, you can see how many visitors became real leads, and which pages and channels produced them.

## The numbers worth watching (and the ones to ignore)

Most dashboards throw dozens of metrics at you, and it's easy to feel buried. For a small business, only a few really earn your attention.

- **Conversions** — form submissions, calls and bookings. This is the single number that maps to money, so track it above all else.
- **Traffic sources** — are visitors arriving from Google search, social media, paid ads or word of mouth? This shows you where your effort is actually paying off.
- **Top landing pages** — which pages pull people in, and which of them turn browsers into enquiries. Your best performers usually deserve more of your attention, not less.
- **Engagement and bounce** — if people leave within seconds, the message, loading speed or [website design](/services/website-design) probably needs a rethink.
- **Mobile versus desktop** — most local searches happen on a phone, so a site that's fiddly on mobile is quietly turning customers away.

## Turn the numbers into decisions

Data is only useful when it changes what you do. Say your analytics show plenty of visitors landing on your services page but almost none filling in the form. That isn't a traffic problem, it's a conversion problem, and the fix might be a clearer heading, a photo of your team or a shorter form. If a slow page is quietly losing mobile visitors, speeding it up could win back enquiries you never knew you were missing.

Check in once a month rather than once a day. Trends over a few weeks tell a far more honest story than a single quiet Tuesday. Keeping a light [care plan](/services/care-plans) in place means someone is watching the numbers even when you're flat out running the business.

> Traffic tells you people are interested. Conversions tell you your website is doing its job.

## Frequently asked questions

### What's the difference between Google Analytics and Search Console?

They answer different questions. Google Search Console shows how people find you — the search terms you appear for and roughly where you rank. Google Analytics 4 shows what they do once they arrive: which pages they visit, how long they stay and whether they get in touch. Most businesses want both.

### How do I know if my website is actually performing well?

Look past total visits. A site is performing when a healthy share of visitors take action — call you, fill in a form or book. Set up conversion tracking so you can see that number, then compare it month to month rather than day to day.
