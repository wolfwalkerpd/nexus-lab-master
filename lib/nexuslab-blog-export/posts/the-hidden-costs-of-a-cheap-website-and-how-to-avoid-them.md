Everyone loves a bargain, and a £99 website or a free drag-and-drop builder can feel like a sensible way to start. But understanding the hidden costs of a cheap website, and how to avoid them, is a conversation worth having before you commit, because the price on the tin is rarely the price you end up paying. A cheap site often sends you the bill later, in lost enquiries, wasted evenings and a full rebuild you never budgeted for.

This isn't a pitch for spending as much as possible. It's an honest look at where the real costs hide, what genuinely drives the price of a small-business website in the UK, and how to spend your money once instead of twice.

## What a cheap website usually looks like

Cheap comes in a few familiar shapes. There's the DIY builder you set up yourself over a weekend. There's the £99 or £149 offer that lands in your inbox. There's the freelancer on a global marketplace charging £80 for a five-page site, and there's the friend of a friend who 'does websites' on the side.

None of these are automatically bad. Plenty of businesses start on a builder and do fine. The trouble begins when a low upfront price hides work that still needs doing, and someone, usually you, ends up doing it or paying for it further down the line.

## The hidden costs of a cheap website, and how to avoid them

The sticker price only covers what you can see. Here are the costs that tend to surface later, and the simple way to sidestep each one.

- **Lost enquiries.** If your site loads slowly, looks dated or is fiddly on a phone, people leave before they ever call you. Most local searches happen on mobile, so a site that struggles on a small screen quietly costs you customers every week.
- **Rebuild fees.** A site built without a plan often has to be scrapped and started again within a year or two. You pay twice, once for the cheap version and again for the one that actually works.
- **Platform lock-in.** Some cheap builders and 'free' packages keep your domain, content and data on their terms. If you decide to leave, you can't easily take your site with you. Always check that you own your site, domain and content outright.
- **Your own time.** DIY looks free until you count the evenings spent wrestling with templates instead of running your business. Time is the cost nobody puts on the invoice.
- **Search invisibility.** A bargain site is often built with no [SEO](/services/seo) foundation at all: no proper structure, no local signals, nothing to help Google understand who you are. You end up invisible to the exact people searching for you.
- **No one to call.** When something breaks the week before a big push, a cheap package rarely comes with a person who picks up the phone.

## What actually drives the price of a website

If cheap has hidden costs, it helps to know what you're really paying for when a price is fair. A website's cost comes down to a handful of things: how many pages and features it needs, whether the design is bespoke or a stock template, who writes the words, and whether the search and technical groundwork is done properly.

As a rough guide, a simple brochure site from a UK freelancer might be a few hundred pounds, while a larger site with custom features, professional copy and proper SEO can run into several thousand. Neither is 'right', it depends on what your business genuinely needs.

## How to avoid paying twice

Avoiding the hidden costs isn't about spending more. It's about asking a few sharp questions before you hand over any money.

Ask who owns the site, domain and content when the work is done, and the answer should always be you. Ask whether the price is fixed or likely to creep. Ask what happens after launch: is there support, or are you on your own the moment it goes live? A build that includes aftercare and a simple way to keep the site healthy, like a straightforward [care plan](/services/care-plans), saves you far more than it costs.

Get those answers in writing and most of the hidden costs simply disappear. You'll pay a fair price once, own what you've paid for, and have someone to call when you need them.
