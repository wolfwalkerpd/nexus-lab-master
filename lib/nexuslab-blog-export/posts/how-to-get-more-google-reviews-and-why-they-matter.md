If you run a local business, Google reviews are quietly deciding whether new customers choose you or the shop down the road. Learning how to get more Google reviews, and why they matter so much, is one of the cheapest ways to win more work without spending a penny on advertising. Yet most small businesses leave it to chance and hope a happy customer remembers to say something nice.

Good reviews are a habit, not a stroke of luck. Here is a plain-English guide to why they pull their weight, and exactly how to ask for them without feeling awkward or pushy.

## Why Google reviews matter more than you'd think

When someone searches for a plumber in Leeds or a dentist in Cardiff, Google shows a short list of nearby businesses with a row of gold stars under each name. Those stars are often the first thing people judge you on, long before they reach your website. A strong set of recent reviews quietly does three jobs at once.

Recency counts too. A dozen reviews from the last few months says far more than fifty reviews that dried up two years ago. That is why getting reviews needs to be an ongoing habit, not a one-off push.

- **Trust.** Most people read reviews before they call, and treat them a bit like a personal recommendation from a friend.
- **Visibility.** Reviews are one of the signals Google uses to decide who appears in the local map results, so they feed straight into your [local SEO](/services/seo).
- **Choice.** Faced with two similar businesses, people almost always pick the one with more reviews and a higher star rating.

> Reviews are the closest thing you have to word of mouth working for you around the clock.

## How to get more Google reviews without feeling pushy

Here is the part most guides skip. You will get far more reviews by making the ask simple, timely and personal. People are usually glad to help a small business they liked — they just need a gentle nudge and an easy route to the review form.

To find your link, open your Google Business Profile, choose 'Ask for reviews' and copy the short URL it gives you. Save it somewhere handy so anyone on your team can share it in seconds.

- **Ask at the right moment.** Just after you have finished a job well, or when a customer says thank you, is when goodwill is highest.
- **Make it one tap.** Send a direct link to your Google review form by text or email, rather than telling people to search for you.
- **Ask in person, then follow up.** A quick verbal request backed by a link a few minutes later converts best.
- **Keep it personal.** A short message from a real person beats a generic automated blast every time.
- **Add gentle reminders.** Put your review link in your email signature, on invoices, receipts and thank-you cards.
- **Never buy or bribe reviews.** Google can spot fake or incentivised reviews and may remove them or penalise your profile.

## Build asking into your everyday routine

The businesses with the best review profiles rarely have a clever trick. They have a habit. Reviews come in steadily because asking is baked into how they finish every job, rather than something they remember to do once a quarter when work is quiet.

Pick one or two natural moments in your day — handing over the keys, sending the final invoice, closing a project call — and attach the ask to them. Print a small QR code that points to your review form and keep it by the till or in the van. Brief everyone on the team so it doesn't rest on one person. Once it becomes routine, a handful of fresh reviews a month looks after itself.

## Reply to every review, good or bad

Reviews are a conversation, not a scoreboard. Thank people for the good ones by name, and answer the critical ones calmly and quickly. A measured reply to a one-star review often impresses future customers more than the complaint itself, because it shows you take problems seriously.

Replying also tells Google your profile is active and looked after, which never hurts. Aim to respond within a day or two while it is still fresh. If a complaint is fair, acknowledge it, put it right where you can, and say what you've changed. If it's unfair or fake, stay polite and factual — future readers can usually tell the difference, and a calm reply always wins.

## Turn your reviews into more customers

Reviews shouldn't just sit on Google. Feature your best ones where buyers are already deciding — on your homepage, service pages and quote forms. Seeing real, recent praise at the moment of choice reassures people and nudges them to get in touch. A short line under each review — the customer's first name and their town — makes it feel genuine without giving away private details.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is it against Google's rules to ask customers for reviews?

No. Google is happy for you to ask customers for honest reviews. What is not allowed is offering discounts or gifts in exchange for them, or only asking your happiest customers. Ask everyone, and let them say what they truly think.

### How many Google reviews do I need?

There is no magic number. What matters most is having more than your nearby competitors, keeping a healthy average, and adding fresh reviews regularly. A steady trickle beats a big one-off burst that then stops.

### How do I get my Google review link?

Sign in to your Google Business Profile, select 'Ask for reviews', and copy the short link. Share that link by text, email or a QR code on receipts so customers reach the review form in one tap.
