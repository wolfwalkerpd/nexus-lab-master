A free website builder can get your business online by the weekend, so it's a fair question: when it comes to **free website builders vs paid, what's the catch**? The honest answer is that free rarely stays free once your website has a real job to do — but it's a more balanced picture than the sales pages on either side will admit.

This is a straight comparison for UK small businesses weighing up a DIY builder like Wix, Squarespace or GoDaddy against a professionally built site. We'll give the free option its due, because for some businesses it genuinely is the right call. Then we'll show you where the costs quietly appear, and how to choose the route that protects your time and your growth.

## What free website builders do well

Let's start by being fair. Platforms like Wix, Squarespace and GoDaddy have spent years making it simple to get a page online without touching a line of code. For a brand-new venture testing an idea, a weekend side hustle, or a market stall that just needs an address and opening hours, that can be exactly enough.

Here's where they earn their keep:

If your website is essentially a digital business card, a builder may serve you perfectly well for a while.

- No large upfront cost, so you can test an idea before you commit
- Drag-and-drop editing you can learn in an afternoon
- Templates that look tidy on a phone straight out of the box
- Hosting, security certificates and updates handled for you
- Quick to publish — you can be live the same day

## Free website builders vs paid: where the catch hides

Now the honest part. With most builders, 'free' is the trial floor rather than the finished product. To remove the platform's branding, connect your own domain, take card payments or unlock anything beyond the basics, you move onto a paid monthly plan — and those plans climb quickly the moment your needs grow.

The subtler catches are the ones that cost you later. Lower-tier plans tend to bolt on transaction fees when you sell, nudge you towards paid add-ons for bookings, email capture or galleries, and give you limited control over the technical foundations that help you rank. Templates also mean your site can end up looking like a dozen competitors down the same high street.

Then there's the cost that never shows on the invoice: your time. Every hour spent wrestling a template into shape is an hour not spent serving customers. And if you ever outgrow the platform, moving away can mean rebuilding from scratch, because your content and design are locked to their system.

> Free is rarely the price you end up paying — it's the price of the version you'll outgrow in six months.

## Adding up the true cost

Add it up over a couple of years and the gap narrows more than you'd expect. A builder that looked free quietly becomes a subscription of its own once you've paid for a plan to remove branding, connected a domain, added a professional email address and switched on a few paid features — and you're still doing the work and living with the limits.

A one-off professional build is a bigger cheque up front, but it's a fixed, known figure, and the finished site is an asset you own rather than something you rent indefinitely. For a business that depends on being found online, that often works out as the cheaper route in the end.

## So which is right for your business?

Neither choice is right in the abstract; it depends on what your website needs to do. Be honest with yourself about that, and the decision usually makes itself.

A builder can be the sensible starting point if you're just testing the water, your budget is near zero, and the site is really a placeholder you're happy to maintain yourself. A professional build tends to pay for itself when your website is a genuine source of enquiries or bookings, when you want to rank for local searches, and when your time is better spent running the business than editing web pages.
