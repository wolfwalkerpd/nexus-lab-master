## How Ealing homeowners actually look for a trade

Ealing is a borough of period homes — long rows of Victorian and Edwardian houses across W5 and W13, plenty of them mid-renovation. That shapes the work: rewires, boiler swaps, loft conversions, side-return extensions and the steady stream of small jobs that older properties throw up. It also shapes the searching. When a boiler dies in January or a fuse board trips on a Sunday, people grab a phone and type 'emergency plumber Ealing' or 'electrician near me', then call one of the first firms that looks trustworthy and actually answers.

Two things follow from that. First, most of these searches happen on a mobile, often while someone stands in a cold kitchen, so a site that's fiddly on a small screen quietly loses the job. Second, the Elizabeth line has filled Ealing Broadway, West Ealing and Hanwell with time-poor commuters who will book the trade that makes it easy and skip the one that leaves them waiting for a callback.

## The features that turn a visit into a booked job

A trades website doesn't need to be clever. It needs to remove every reason a busy homeowner might hesitate. The parts that consistently earn their keep are the simple ones:

None of that is showy. All of it is the difference between a site that merely gets looked at and a [website that actually books work](/services/website-design).

- A tap-to-call button that stays fixed at the top of the screen, so nobody has to hunt for your number.
- A short quote or callback form — name, postcode, job, done — not a twenty-field questionnaire.
- A photo gallery of real work you've done nearby: a Hanwell kitchen, a Pitshanger loft, a rewired flat off the Uxbridge Road.
- Genuine Google reviews shown on the page, so trust is doing its job before you even speak.
- Clear service and area pages, so 'boiler repair' and 'Perivale' both have somewhere to land from search.
- Fast loading on 4G, because a site that stalls for a few seconds has already lost a chunk of its visitors.
