If you run a local business, the choice often comes down to this: build it yourself on Squarespace, or commission a studio for a bespoke website. Both can look smart. Both can bring in enquiries. But they suit different businesses at different stages, and the wrong call can cost you months of lost search traffic. Here is a fair, plain-English look at Squarespace vs a bespoke website, so you can choose with your eyes open.

## What Squarespace does well

Let's start by being fair to Squarespace, because it has real strengths. For a brand-new business testing an idea, it is quick, tidy and affordable. You get attractive templates, hosting bundled in, and an editor you can learn in an afternoon. If you sell a handful of products or just need a simple presence online, it will do the job without a big upfront bill.

For a lot of side projects, market traders and early-stage founders, that is exactly the right call. There is no sense commissioning a custom build for something you are still shaping. Squarespace lets you get online this week and change your mind next month, and that flexibility has genuine value when nothing is settled yet.

Where Squarespace starts to strain is when your ambitions grow. The templates that made setup easy become the ceiling you keep bumping into, and the monthly fee keeps ticking over whether the site is earning its keep or not.

## Where a bespoke website earns its keep

A [bespoke website](/services/website-design) is built around your business, not squeezed into someone else's template. That difference shows up exactly where it matters to a serious local business: how fast your pages load, how well they rank, and how easily the site handles the specific way you win customers.

Think of a plumber who gets most of his work from 'emergency plumber near me', or a dental practice competing with three others on the same high street. Winning those searches depends on clean code, fast pages and a structure built for [local SEO](/services/seo). Template builders get you part of the way, but you are always working within what the platform allows. A custom build gives you the room to do it properly.

There is also the matter of standing out. When a dozen firms in your town all pick from the same popular templates, your site starts to look like everyone else's. A design made for your brand, with copy written around the services you actually want to sell, does quiet work every day to make you the obvious choice.

> A bespoke site is built around how your business actually wins customers — not around a template's limits.

## Squarespace vs a Bespoke Website: The Trade-offs

No option wins on everything. Here is how the two stack up on the things local business owners tell us matter most:

- **Upfront cost**: Squarespace is cheaper to start; a bespoke build is a larger one-off investment that you own outright.
- **Ongoing cost**: Squarespace charges a subscription for as long as you use it; a custom site runs on low-cost hosting, with an optional [care plan](/services/care-plans) only if you want hands-off support.
- **Speed and SEO**: templates carry code you'll never use, which can slow pages; a hand-built site is leaner and simpler to optimise.
- **Design**: templates look polished but familiar; a bespoke design reflects your brand and can be shaped around your best services.
- **Ownership**: on Squarespace you rent the platform; with a custom build you own the site, the domain and the data, with no lock-in.
- **Room to grow**: booking systems, memberships or unusual features are simple to add to a bespoke site and often awkward on a template.

## The real cost, over time

It is tempting to compare only the upfront price, but the honest comparison is what each option costs over three or four years. Squarespace looks cheaper on day one, then keeps charging every month for as long as your site exists. A bespoke build is a bigger cheque up front, after which your running costs can be modest — hosting and, if you want it, a maintenance plan.

For a hobby site, the subscription barely registers. For a growing business, those monthly fees add up while you are still boxed in by the platform's limits. When your website is winning you customers, paying once for something you own and control often works out both cheaper and more profitable in the long run.

## So which should you choose?

The honest answer is that it depends on where your business is right now.

If you are testing an idea, watching every pound, and happy to build it yourself, start with Squarespace. There is no shame in it, and you can always move up later.

If your website is a real source of income — if enquiries, bookings or sales genuinely depend on it — a bespoke build usually pays for itself. You get a faster site, stronger search performance and a design that sets you apart from competitors using the very same templates. Still unsure? That is normal — most owners are weighing budget against ambition. The useful question is not 'which is better?' but 'which is right for where my business is heading over the next two years?'

## Can you switch later?

Plenty of the owners we work with started on a DIY builder and outgrew it, so this is not a decision you are locked into forever. Your domain and content can come with you, and a well-planned migration protects the rankings you have already earned. Moving sooner rather than later simply means less to redo — and less time spent limited by a template while your competitors pull ahead.

## The quick wins

- Squarespace is a strong, low-cost start for testing an idea or running a simple online presence.
- A bespoke website wins on speed, SEO control, design and true ownership — which matters once the site drives real income.
- Choose by stage: DIY while you are figuring it out, custom when enquiries and bookings depend on your site.
- Compare cost over three to four years, not just day one — subscriptions add up while a site you own keeps running cheaply.
