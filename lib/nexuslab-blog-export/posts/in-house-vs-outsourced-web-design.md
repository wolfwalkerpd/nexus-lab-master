The in house vs outsourced web design question comes up for almost every growing business at some point. Maybe someone on your team is handy with WordPress. Maybe your nephew built a site for his band and it looked decent. Or maybe you're staring at a quiet enquiry inbox and wondering whether the fix is a person you hire or a studio you call.

Both routes can work. The right answer depends on your budget, your timescale, and how much of your own week you can realistically give to a project like this. Here is an honest look at each, so you can choose with your eyes open rather than on a gut feeling.

## What in-house web design really means

Building in-house means the work stays inside your business. That might be a dedicated marketing hire, an existing team member who picks it up alongside their day job, or you at the kitchen table on a Sunday night. The appeal is obvious: nobody understands your business like the people already in it, and changes can happen the moment you think of them.

The catch is that a good website is a set of skills, not one skill. You need design, copywriting, technical build, SEO, accessibility and ongoing upkeep. It is rare for one person to do all of that well, and rarer still for them to have spare hours once the site is live and the day job resumes.

- Full control and instant tweaks, with no waiting on a third party
- Deep knowledge of your products, customers and tone
- Salary, software and training costs that add up quickly for one project
- A real risk the site stalls half-built when busy season hits

## What outsourcing gives you

Outsourcing means handing the build to a studio or freelancer who does this every day. You bring the knowledge of your business; they bring the craft of turning it into a site that loads fast, reads well and shows up in search. The trade-off people worry about is control, but a good partner works with you, not around you.

The quality here varies wildly, which is fair to admit. A cheap overseas template farm and a UK studio that answers the phone are both technically outsourcing, and they produce very different results. What you are really buying is judgement: someone who has seen what works for a business like yours and can steer you past the expensive mistakes.

- A team of specialists instead of one stretched generalist
- Faster delivery because it is their full-time focus
- Fixed costs you can plan around, rather than an open-ended salary
- Someone to maintain the site so it doesn't quietly rot

## The hidden costs people forget

When most people compare the two, they compare an agency quote against a salary or a DIY afternoon. But the true cost of in-house is rarely just the wage. There is the software, the training, the plugins, the hours your team spends not doing the thing you actually hired them for. A site that takes an in-house novice four months might take a studio four weeks, and those extra three months are three months of lost enquiries.

There is also the maintenance question, which quietly eats businesses alive. A website is not a painting you hang and forget. It needs security updates, content changes, broken links fixed and the odd redesign as your services grow. Without a plan for that, even a beautiful site drifts out of date. This is where a simple monthly arrangement like a [care plan](/services/care-plans) earns its keep.

> A website is not a painting you hang and forget. The build is the first month; the upkeep is the next five years.

## When each option makes sense

To be fair to both sides, there are cases where keeping it in-house is the smart call. If you have a genuine specialist on staff with the time to match, or you are testing a brand-new idea and just need something live this week, an in-house build can be quicker and cheaper. There is no shame in a tidy DIY site while you find your feet.

But for an established local business that depends on its website to bring in work, the maths usually favours a professional build. You get the whole skill set, a predictable price and a site built to be found. On paper it can look cheaper, but the honest answer is often no once you count everything. A salary or your own time, software subscriptions, training and the months a half-built site sits unfinished all add up. A fixed-price studio build often costs less overall and gets you earning sooner, because the site is live in weeks rather than seasons.
