If you've ever wondered why one plumber ranks above another for the exact same search, backlinks are a big part of the answer. Understanding how backlinks work and how to earn them is one of the most useful things a small-business owner can learn about SEO, and one of the most misunderstood. This guide explains it in plain English, with no jargon and no shortcuts that will land you in trouble.

A backlink is simply a link from another website to yours. Google treats those links a bit like recommendations: when a trusted site points at you, it's quietly vouching for you. Get enough good ones, from the right places, and you become easier to find. Here's how the whole thing works, and how a local business can earn links the honest way.

## What a backlink actually is

Picture the web as one enormous network of roads, and links as the roads that connect sites together. A backlink is any link on someone else's website that leads back to yours: a mention in a local news article, an entry in a trade directory, a supplier who lists you as an approved installer, or a blog that quotes your advice.

Every one of those links does two jobs at once. It sends real people to your site, and it tells search engines that another website found you worth mentioning. The first job matters on its own. A link from a busy regional paper or a local Facebook group can bring in enquiries whether or not it ever moves you up Google.

## How backlinks work: it's about trust, not numbers

Search engines can't walk into your shop or read the room the way a customer does. So they look for signals that other people already trust you, and links are one of the strongest signals there is. In simple terms, each quality backlink acts like a vote. A site with lots of genuine votes from respected sources looks more authoritative, and Google is more likely to show it near the top of the results.

But it is not a simple headcount. A single link from a well-known local newspaper is worth far more than fifty links from spammy directories nobody reads. Relevance matters too. A link from a wedding blog means far more to a florist than a link from a car-parts forum. Quality and relevance beat raw numbers every time.

> One link from a site people actually trust is worth more than a hundred from sites they don't.

## What makes a backlink worth having

Not all links carry the same weight. When we review a site's links in a teardown, these are the things that separate a link worth having from one that's useless, or even harmful:

- **Relevance** — the linking site is about your industry, your town, or something clearly related to what you do.
- **Trust** — it's an established site with a real audience, not a directory built purely to sell links.
- **Context** — the link sits inside genuine content, with natural wording around it, rather than buried in a footer stuffed with hundreds of others.
- **A proper editorial link** — most links in real articles pass value by default; some, like social posts, are tagged so they pass less.
- **Placement you didn't pay for** — earned links read as recommendations, while bought ones can get you penalised.

## How to earn backlinks as a small business

You don't need a big budget or a PR agency to earn good links. Most local businesses earn their best ones simply by being genuinely useful and easy to reference. Here are approaches that work well for a UK small business:

- **Get listed properly** — your Google Business Profile, plus trusted directories like Yell, Checkatrade or your trade body. Keep your name, address and phone number identical everywhere.
- **Be a local supplier or member** — chambers of commerce, town BID websites, and the sports clubs or events you sponsor will often link back to you.
- **Create something worth citing** — a genuinely useful guide, an honest price breakdown, or a bit of local data. People link to things that answer a real question.
- **Offer a quote to local press** — regional papers and community blogs are always short of content and happy to credit a nearby expert who makes their job easier.
- **Ask the partners you already work with** — the tradesperson you subcontract to, the accountant you recommend, the venue you supply. A real, mutual mention is completely fair game.

## The links to avoid

It's tempting to buy a bundle of '500 backlinks for £30' when the advert pops up. Don't. Google has spent more than twenty years getting good at spotting bought and automated links, and at best they do nothing at all. At worst, a bad batch of links can drag your whole site down and take months of careful work to clean up.

The same goes for link farms, hidden footer links spread across dozens of unrelated sites, and comment spam. If a link exists only to game the algorithm, assume Google can tell. Slow, honest links beat a quick bundle that puts your site at risk, which is why we build our [SEO work](/services/seo) around content and reputation rather than shortcuts.
