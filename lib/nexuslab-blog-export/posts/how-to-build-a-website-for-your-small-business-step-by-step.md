Figuring out how to build a website for your small business can feel bigger than it actually is. You do not need to be technical, and you do not need a big budget to start. What you need is a clear plan and the right order to do things in. This guide walks you through it step by step, in plain English, for a UK small business that just wants to get found and get more work.

We build sites for a living, so we will be straight with you about what you can do yourself and where it is worth paying for help. Either way, by the end of this article you will know exactly what a good small-business website needs, and roughly what each step involves.

## Start with the job your website needs to do

Before you pick a template or a colour, get clear on the one thing you want your website to do. A plumber in Leeds wants the phone to ring. A café in Bristol wants people to find the opening hours and book a table. A bookkeeper wants tidy enquiries from businesses nearby. Write that single main action on a sticky note, then build everything around it. Skip this step and you end up with a good-looking site that quietly brings in no work at all.

It sounds obvious, but it is the part most people rush. Get it right and every later decision, from the menu to the buttons, becomes easier to make.

## Sort your domain name and hosting

Your domain is your address on the web, something like yourbusiness.co.uk. A .co.uk suits most UK businesses and gently signals that you are local, which matters when nearby customers are choosing who to trust. Hosting is simply where your site's files live so people can reach them around the clock. Many builders bundle the two together, which keeps life simple when you are starting out.

One rule is worth following without exception: register the domain in your own name, never an agency's or a friend's. If you ever move or fall out, you want to take your address, your site and your data with you. We think that ownership should be yours by default, with no lock-in.

## Should you build a website for your small business yourself?

This is the big fork in the road, and the right answer depends on your time, your budget and how much you plan to grow. There are three realistic routes.

There is no shame in any of them. If you value your time and want it done properly once, our [website design and build](/services/website-design) service takes the whole job off your plate, from copy to launch.

- Website builders like Wix or Squarespace are the quickest to start and cheap month to month. Fine for a simple presence, but they can box you in as your needs grow.
- WordPress is more flexible and tends to do better on Google over time, but there is a real learning curve and you handle the updates, backups and security yourself.
- A small studio costs more up front, yet you get a site built around your goals and a real person to ring when something breaks.

> The best website is the one you will actually keep up to date, not the one with the most features.

## Plan your pages and write copy that sounds like you

Most local sites need only a handful of pages: a home page, a services page, an about page and an easy way to get in touch. If you serve several towns or trades, a dedicated area or [industry page](/industries) can help you show up for those specific searches. Do not overbuild it; a tight, clear site beats a sprawling one every time.

Write for a real person, not a search engine. Say plainly what you do, where you work, roughly what it costs, and how to reach you. A simple from price saves everyone time and filters out the wrong enquiries. Use real photos of your team, your van or your shopfront too; they beat stock images every time and build trust fast.

## Get your site found with mobile and local SEO

Most local searches happen on a phone, so your site has to load quickly and be easy to tap with a thumb. If people have to pinch and zoom to read your prices, they leave. Test the whole thing on your own mobile before you worry about anything clever.

Getting found then comes down to a few honest habits: claim and fill in your Google Business Profile, give each page a clear title, gather genuine reviews, and mention the places you serve. This is the heart of [local SEO](/services/seo), and it rewards patience, paying off over months rather than days. Be wary of anyone promising you the top spot by next week.

## The quick wins

- Decide the one action you want visitors to take before you build anything.
- Own your domain and data yourself, so you are never locked in.
- Match the build method to your time and budget: builder, WordPress, or a studio.
- Local SEO and mobile speed are what get you found; both reward patience.
- Launch is the beginning, not the end; regular upkeep keeps a site working and safe.
