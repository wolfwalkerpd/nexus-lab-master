If you have ever asked how much a website costs in the UK, you have probably found the internet unhelpfully vague. One page says £200, the next says £20,000, and neither explains why. The honest answer is that a website is priced a bit like a kitchen: the size, the finish and who fits it all move the number. This guide gives you real 2026 ranges, explains what you are actually paying for, and helps you spot when a quote is fair.

We build sites for UK small businesses every week, so these are the numbers we see in the real world — not headline figures dressed up to look cheap. By the end you will know roughly what to budget, what pushes the price up, and how to get a site that earns its keep rather than just sitting there looking pretty.

## So, how much does a website cost in the UK?

Here are the ranges we see across the UK market in 2026. A do-it-yourself builder like Wix or Squarespace runs from around £10 to £30 a month, plus a fair chunk of your own time. A freelancer will usually charge somewhere between £500 and £3,000 for a small-business site. A small studio or agency typically sits between £1,500 and £6,000 for a polished, professionally built site, and larger agencies working with bigger brands start at £10,000 and climb from there.

For context, our own [fixed-price websites](/pricing) start at £477 for a Starter site and £1,170 for a Growth build, so a professional site does not have to mean five figures. The wide spread comes down to what goes into the work — which is where most quotes stay quiet.

## What actually drives the price

Two quotes can look miles apart and both be honest, because they are describing different amounts of work. These are the things that move the number the most:

- Number of pages — a five-page brochure site costs far less than a forty-page site with locations, services and a blog.
- Custom design versus a template — bespoke branding and layouts take more time than restyling a ready-made theme.
- Features — booking systems, online payments, member logins and multiple languages all add build time.
- Copywriting and photography — words and images that sell are worth paying for, and plenty of cheap quotes quietly leave them out.
- SEO groundwork — a site built to be found on Google is structured differently from one that only has to look nice.

## Cheap is not always cheap

It is tempting to pick the lowest number and move on. But a bargain site that loads slowly, reads badly or never shows up in search is not really a saving — it is a slow leak. Most local searches happen on mobile, and if your site is clumsy on a phone, people simply tap back and ring the next business on the list.

The better question is not how little can I spend, but how much is a steady stream of enquiries worth. Spend enough to do the job properly, and no more than that.

> A £300 website that nobody finds is far more expensive than a £1,500 one that brings you customers.

## How to choose the right budget for your business

Start with what you want the site to do — bring in enquiries, take bookings, sell products — and match the budget to that goal rather than the other way round. A quiet local trade with five services needs something very different from a growing shop selling nationwide, and paying for either extreme by mistake is where money gets wasted.
