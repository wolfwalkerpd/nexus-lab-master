Ask three UK web designers for a quote and you'll get three very different numbers. One says £300, another says £3,000, and a third quietly mentions £10,000. It's enough to make you wonder whether anyone's telling the truth. So here is web design pricing explained in plain English: what you're really paying for, why the numbers swing so wildly, and how to tell a fair price from a bad one.

The short version is that a website isn't one thing. It's design, words, build, testing, and the ongoing job of keeping your site fast and found on Google. Price mostly tracks scope and skill, not a fixed shopping list. Once you can see what sits behind the number, quotes stop feeling like a lottery and you can buy with confidence.

## Why two websites can cost £500 or £5,000

A brochure site for a local plumber and a booking system for a busy dental practice are both called 'websites', but they're miles apart in work. The gap usually comes down to a few honest differences: how many pages you need, whether the design is a template or built for your brand, who writes the words, and how much thought goes into getting you found. A cheap site often skips the last two entirely. That's not a scam, it's just a smaller job. The trouble starts when the invoice looks the same but the work behind it doesn't.

There's also who you're buying from. A large agency carries account managers, offices and overheads that end up in your quote. A one-person freelancer is cheaper, but can go quiet the moment they get busy. A small studio sits in between: senior people doing the actual work, without the corporate mark-up, and close enough to pick up the phone when you call.

- **Strategy and structure** — working out what pages you need, what each should say, and the path you want visitors to take.

## The costs that show up after launch

The sticker price is only half the story. Hosting, domain renewal, security updates, small content changes and the occasional fix all cost either money or time. Some providers keep those fees low by locking you into their own platform, so leaving means rebuilding from scratch. We don't work that way: you own your site, your domain and your data, and you can take them elsewhere whenever you like.

This is where a simple [care plan](/services/care-plans) earns its place. For a fixed monthly amount, updates, backups and small tweaks are handled, so a broken contact form or an out-of-date price doesn't sit there quietly costing you enquiries for weeks. Whether you pay someone or do it yourself, budget for the after, not just the build.

## How to compare quotes without getting stung

The cheapest quote and the most expensive one are rarely the right one. Judge on what's actually included, not the headline figure:

If you've got a quote in front of you and you're not sure it's fair, the fastest way to find out is a second opinion. A free teardown is exactly that: a short, screen-recorded review of your current site, pointing out what's working, what's costing you enquiries, and what a fix would really involve. No pressure, no jargon.

- **Ask what's included** — copywriting, SEO, revisions and training are often extras hidden below the line.
- **Check who owns what** — you should keep your domain, hosting login and files, full stop.
- **Find out who does the work** — the person you meet in the sales chat isn't always the one building your site.
- **Look past launch** — ask what changes cost and how quickly you'll actually get help.
