Most small-business websites go live in three to six weeks. But the real question is how long does it take to build a website for your business, and the honest answer is that it depends less on the design than on you. The biggest delays almost never come from the code. They come from waiting on content, decisions and sign-off.

If you run a busy plumbing firm, a dental practice or a corner café, that's actually good news, because it means the timeline is largely in your hands. Here's where the weeks really go, what tends to hold a project up, and how to get a site live without letting it drag on for months.

## So how long does it take to build a website?

For a typical local-business site — a handful of pages, a contact form, maybe a booking link and a gallery of your work — three to six weeks from kickoff to launch is realistic. A single-page site can be quicker. A bigger site with lots of services, an online shop or custom features will take longer.

That range assumes one important thing: the words and pictures are ready, or someone is writing them for you. A five-page site can be built in a matter of days. It's rarely the building that takes six weeks; it's everything that feeds into it.

## A realistic week-by-week timeline

Here's how a straightforward local-business build tends to play out. Yours may vary, but the shape is usually similar:

- **Week 1 — Planning.** We agree the pages, the structure and what each one needs to say. You send over your logo, photos and any text you already have.
- **Weeks 2–3 — Design and build.** We design the homepage, get your sign-off, then build the rest to match. You watch it take shape on a private link.
- **Week 4 — Content and review.** Real words, real images, your feedback, and the small tweaks that make it feel like yours.
- **Weeks 5–6 — Testing and launch.** We check it on phones and tablets, connect your domain, set up the basics of [local SEO](/services/seo), then go live.

## What actually slows a website down

When a project runs late, it's almost never the design tool's fault. Four things cause most of the delay:

- **Content.** Writing the words, gathering photos, digging the logo out of an old email. This is the number-one hold-up, every single time.
- **Decisions.** Sign-off on the design, the wording, the colours. One decision-maker moves fast; a committee does not.
- **Third parties.** Your domain registrar, your old host, a booking or payment tool that needs connecting. These add days you didn't plan for.
- **Scope creep.** The "while we're at it, can we also..." requests. Each idea is reasonable on its own, but they stack up quickly.

> Only some of these are about design. Most are about content and decisions — which is why a clear brief up front saves weeks at the end.

## When it takes longer than six weeks

Some projects genuinely need more time, and it's worth knowing which. An online shop with products, stock and payments is a bigger job than a brochure site. A site in several languages, a members' area or heavy custom design will each add weeks. So will a full rebrand happening at the same time — new logo, new photography, new name — because the website has to wait on all of it.

None of that is a problem as long as it's planned. The trouble starts when a quick five-page site quietly grows into something twice the size halfway through. Agreeing the scope honestly at the start is what keeps the finish line where you can see it.
