If you run an accountancy practice, a law firm or a consultancy in Greenwich, your website is doing a job long before you ever pick up the phone. Good websites for accountants in Greenwich — and for solicitors and consultants — do one thing above all else: they make a stranger feel safe handing over their tax return, their contract or their business plan. That trust is built in seconds, on a small screen, often before your name is even said aloud.

Professional services are not like the plumber down the road. Nobody chooses an accountant on a whim. They read, they compare two or three firms, they check who else you work with, and only then do they decide whether you look like the sort of practice that answers the phone. This guide walks through what a Greenwich firm's site needs to earn that decision — and where most of them quietly fall short.

## Why websites for accountants in Greenwich have to lead with trust

Greenwich is not short of professional firms. Walk up from the DLR at Cutty Sark toward the town centre, or over to the quieter streets around Blackheath, and you pass accountants, solicitors and small consultancies tucked above shops and inside converted townhouses. Many have traded for years on reputation and referral. The catch is that referral now ends with a search — someone is handed your name, then looks you up before they ever call.

When they do, your website is **the interview**. A dated design, a page that stalls on mobile, or vague copy that could belong to any firm in the country all plant the same quiet doubt: are these people still on top of things? For a practice that handles other people's money, contracts or compliance, that doubt is expensive. A [clear, fast website](/services/website-design) does the opposite — it signals, without saying a word, that you are current, organised and easy to deal with.

> For a firm that handles other people's money, a tidy, up-to-date website isn't vanity — it's the first piece of due diligence a client does on you.

## What clients in Greenwich look for before they call

People choosing a professional adviser in South East London tend to shortlist two or three firms and weigh them side by side. They are rarely looking for something clever. They are looking for **reasons to feel safe**. Give them these, high on the page and repeated through the site:

- A plain statement of what you do and who you help — 'Chartered accountants for Greenwich small businesses and landlords', not a vague line about 'solutions'.
- Proof you are real and local: a Greenwich or Blackheath address, a map, and a landline alongside the mobile.
- Credentials that matter in your field — ICAEW, ACCA, SRA regulation, professional indemnity — shown plainly, not buried three clicks deep.
- Recent, genuine reviews with a first name and a business, so a nervous first-timer sees people like them.
- One obvious next step on every page: call, email or book a short chat, with no hunting required.

## Make your services and fees legible

The firms that win online are usually the ones brave enough to be specific. A visitor should be able to tell within a few seconds whether you handle their situation — self-assessment, limited company accounts, a commercial lease, an employment dispute, a growth plan. Break your services into named pages rather than one long list, so someone searching for a single thing lands exactly where they need to be.

You do not have to publish a full price list, but silence on cost makes people assume the worst. A starting figure, a fixed monthly fee, or an honest 'most clients pay between X and Y' removes the fear that stops an enquiry before it starts. The same clarity helps you get found — a page written plainly around a real service is far easier for Google to place than a page of professional throat-clearing. If ranking locally matters to you, that groundwork is what [local SEO](/services/seo) builds on.

## Easy contact and a genuinely local feel

Your catchment is tighter than you might think. A Greenwich firm's clients mostly come from the same postcodes — SE10 and the streets around it, Blackheath, Charlton, Deptford, and increasingly the new flats going up across Greenwich Peninsula, where a wave of young professionals and small limited companies now need advisers on their doorstep. Speak to that. Name the areas you serve. Mention that clients can reach you easily from the DLR or Greenwich station, or that you take video calls for the ones who would rather not travel.

Then make contact effortless. A short form, a phone number that dials with one tap on mobile, and a clear note of your opening hours will always beat a contact page that asks for a life story. Most people will look you up on a phone, on the move — often the same evening they were handed your name — so the whole journey from landing to enquiry has to survive a small screen and a distracted reader.
