Core Web Vitals sound like something only a developer needs to worry about. They are not. If you run a small business in the UK, these three simple measurements shape how many people stay on your site, pick up the phone, or quietly tap back to Google. Here are Core Web Vitals explained for small businesses, without the jargon.

## What are Core Web Vitals?

Core Web Vitals are three scores Google uses to measure how your website feels to a real visitor. Not how it looks in a designer's mockup, but how it behaves on an average phone with an average connection. There are three of them, and each one measures a different kind of frustration.

Put simply: does the page load quickly, respond quickly, and stay still while it does it. That is the whole idea.

- **Largest Contentful Paint (LCP)** — how long the main content takes to show up. A good score is under 2.5 seconds.
- **Interaction to Next Paint (INP)** — how quickly the page reacts when someone taps a button or opens a menu. Aim for under 200 milliseconds.
- **Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS)** — how much the page jumps around as it loads. You want a score below 0.1, so nothing moves under a visitor's thumb.

## Why Core Web Vitals matter for a small business

You might think this is a technical box-ticking exercise. It is not. Most local searches happen on a mobile, often outside on patchy data, and people decide in a second or two whether to wait. A slow or jumpy page costs you enquiries before anyone has read a word about you.

There is a search angle too. Google uses page experience as one of many ranking signals, so genuinely fast sites have a small edge over slow ones. It will not outrank strong, relevant content on its own, but when two plumbers or two cafes are otherwise even, the faster site tends to win the click. Good speed also supports the rest of your [SEO](/services/seo) work rather than quietly undermining it.

> A visitor who waits four seconds for your page to load has usually already tapped back to Google.

## How to check your own site

You do not need to pay anyone to find out where you stand. Google's PageSpeed Insights is free: type in your web address and it grades each of the three vitals, on mobile and desktop, using real-world data where it has it. If you have Google Search Console set up, its Core Web Vitals report shows which pages pass and which need work across your whole site.

Test on mobile first, because that is where most of your visitors are. And test a few different pages, not just the homepage, since a heavy service page or photo gallery often scores far worse than the front door.

## Why small-business sites usually fail these

When a local site scores badly, it is nearly always the same handful of culprits. The good news is that none of them are mysterious, and most are fixable.

- **Huge, unoptimised images** straight off a phone or camera, weighing several megabytes each.
- **Cheap, overloaded shared hosting** that responds slowly before your page has even started to load.
- **Too many plugins and tracking scripts** bolted on over the years, each one adding weight.
- **Images and ads with no set size**, so the layout jumps as they load in — the main cause of a poor CLS score.
- **Bloated page-builder templates** that ship far more code than your site actually uses.

## What you can actually do about it

You do not have to become a developer. Start with the cheap wins: compress your images before uploading, remove plugins and scripts you no longer use, and set a width and height on images so the page stops shifting. Those three alone move most sites in the right direction.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are Core Web Vitals in plain English?

They are three scores Google uses to judge how your website feels to a visitor: how fast the main content loads (LCP), how quickly the page responds to a tap or click (INP), and how much it jumps around while loading (CLS). In short, is it quick, responsive and steady.

### Do Core Web Vitals affect my Google ranking?

They help, but they are only one of many ranking signals. A fast, stable site has a small edge, especially on mobile, but it will not outrank genuinely better, more relevant content on speed alone. Treat good vitals as support for your content, not a replacement for it.
