If you sell online, or you're about to, one question tends to come before all the others: how much does an e-commerce website cost to build? The honest answer is that it depends, but not in the vague, hand-waving way agencies often mean it. The price is driven by a handful of specific choices, and once you understand them you can tell a fair quote from an inflated one.

This guide gives you realistic UK ranges, explains what actually moves the number, and shows where a small business can spend sensibly. No jargon, no scare tactics, just figures and the reasoning behind them.

## What you're actually paying for

An online shop is far more than a few product pages. When you pay for one, you're paying for design, the store setup, secure payments, and the quiet plumbing that keeps orders flowing without you noticing. Skimp on the wrong part and it shows at the worst possible moment: the checkout.

Most [e-commerce website design](/services/website-design) breaks down into a few core pieces, and it helps to see them laid out before you compare any quotes:

- Design and branding, so your shop looks trustworthy enough for someone to hand over their card details
- Store build, meaning product pages, categories, search and a basket that works properly on a phone
- Payments and tax, with card processing, VAT, delivery rules and refunds all set up correctly
- Content, which is your product photos, descriptions and the copy that does the actual selling
- Launch and testing, so orders, emails and stock levels all behave before you go live

## How much does an e-commerce website cost to build in the UK?

As a rough guide, here is what the main routes tend to cost in 2026. Treat these as market ranges rather than quotes, because every shop is a little different and the figure moves with what you sell.

Where you land depends less on the price tag itself and more on what you're selling, and to how many people. A small shop with twenty products has very different needs to one juggling thousands of lines and daily stock updates.

- Doing it yourself on Shopify or Wix: around £20 to £60 a month for the platform, plus a good deal of your own time
- A freelancer using a template: roughly £800 to £2,500 for a straightforward shop
- A small studio building something tailored: roughly £2,500 to £10,000, depending on features
- A large agency or complex custom build: £10,000 and up

> A shop that pays for itself is a bargain at any of these figures. A cheap one that spooks people at checkout costs you every single day it's live.

## Where small businesses overspend

Plenty of owners pay for things they will never use. The classic mistakes are buying a huge platform built for hundreds of thousands of orders, commissioning custom design for pages a template would handle beautifully, and signing open-ended contracts where the bill quietly climbs by the hour.

The other silent cost is what happens after launch. A shop needs updating, backups and the occasional fix. A simple [care plan](/services/care-plans) at a fixed monthly rate is almost always cheaper than emergency call-outs when something breaks during a busy sale, and it saves you the stress of scrambling for help when you can least afford the downtime.
