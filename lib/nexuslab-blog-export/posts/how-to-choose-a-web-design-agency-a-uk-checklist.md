Choosing the wrong web design agency costs you twice: once when you pay for the site, and again when you pay someone else to fix it. This guide on how to choose a web design agency is a plain UK checklist for busy small-business owners — no jargon, no sales pressure.

If you have never hired for this before, the market can feel like a fog of vague quotes and promises that sound too good to be true. Below are the questions to ask, the warning signs to walk away from, and an honest note on when it makes sense to do it yourself.

## Get clear on what you need first

Before you speak to a single agency, spend twenty minutes writing down what the site is actually for. A dentist in Leeds who wants more implant enquiries needs something different from a plumber in Bristol who mainly wants the phone to ring. Your goal shapes everything — the pages, the copy, the calls to action, even the platform.

You do not need a technical spec. You need clarity on three things: the one action you want visitors to take, the handful of pages you genuinely need, and a rough budget. An agency worth hiring will take that plain brief and ask sharper questions, not bury you in acronyms. If you want a steer on what a small-business site should include, our [website design service](/services/website-design) lays it out without the tech-speak.

## Your UK checklist for choosing a web design agency

Once you have a shortlist of two or three agencies, ask every one of them the same questions. Asking the same things is the only way to compare like for like, because a polished sales pitch can hide a very ordinary service.

- **Who owns the site, domain and content when it is finished?** The answer should always be you — no exceptions.
- **Is the price fixed, or billed by the hour?** Fixed pricing means you know the full cost before any work begins.
- **Who will I actually deal with, and can I ring them?** A small team that answers the phone beats a faceless ticket queue.
- **What happens after launch?** Ask about training, aftercare, and whether you can update the site yourself.
- **Can I see real work for businesses like mine?** Ask for live UK sites you can click through, not just screenshots.
- **How long until it goes live, and what do you need from me to hit that date?**

> The single most important question is the simplest: when this is finished, do I own everything?

## Red flags that should make you walk away

Some warning signs are worth walking away from even when the price looks tempting. Be wary of anyone who promises to get you ranked number one on Google — no honest agency can guarantee that, and Google itself says so.

Watch for vague pricing that only firms up after you have signed, contracts that quietly lock your domain or content to the agency, and portfolios full of identical templates with only the town name swapped in. If they cannot show you a live site and a real person who worked on it, keep looking. ## What a fair price actually looks like

Price is where most small-business owners feel out of their depth, so it helps to know the shape of the market. In the UK, a simple, well-built small-business website typically runs from a few hundred pounds for a starter site to a few thousand for something larger with custom design and SEO built in.

What matters more than the headline number is what sits behind it. Ask whether the quote is fixed or an estimate that can creep. Ask what is included — copywriting, images, training, aftercare — and what costs extra later. ## Do it yourself, or hand it over?

You can absolutely build a small site yourself with a website builder, and for a brand-new venture testing an idea, that is often the sensible first step. The honest trade-off is time. A DIY site can eat your evenings for weeks and still look like a DIY site to the customer deciding whether to trust you.

Hiring an agency buys you speed, a professional finish, and someone to call when something breaks. ## The quick wins

- Get clear on your one goal and rough budget before you shortlist anyone.
- Insist on owning your site, domain and content — and on fixed pricing you can see up front.
- Walk away from guaranteed-ranking promises and template portfolios with the town name swapped in.
- Compare two or three agencies on the same questions, then book a teardown to sense-check your choice.

### Should I choose a local agency or a national one?

Neither is automatically better. What matters is whether they understand UK small businesses, answer the phone, and can show you live local sites. A small UK-based team you deal with directly often beats a large agency where you become a ticket number.

### What questions should I ask a web design agency?

Ask who owns the finished site, whether the price is fixed or hourly, who you will actually deal with, what happens after launch, and to see live work for businesses like yours. Ask the same questions of every agency so you can compare fairly.
