## What website maintenance actually covers

Maintenance is the quiet work that keeps a site fast, safe and accurate. ## What pushes the cost up or down

Two businesses can pay very different amounts for perfectly good reasons. A few things move the needle more than the rest.

The platform matters most. A simple, well-built site is cheap to keep healthy; a WordPress site stacked with plugins needs more attention, and an online shop taking payments needs the most. How often you change things matters too — if you post offers every week you will use more time than a site that barely changes from one year to the next. Traffic and security raise the bar as well, because a busier site is a bigger target and needs tighter monitoring. Finally, whether ongoing [SEO](/services/seo) is bundled in changes the number, since optimising a site month after month is real work, not a switch you flick once and forget.

## DIY, a freelancer, or a care plan?

You have three broad options, and the right one depends on your time and your nerves. Doing it yourself is free until it isn't — a lost afternoon wrestling with a broken update is rarely worth the saving. A freelancer on an ad-hoc basis can be excellent value, as long as they actually answer when something breaks at the worst possible moment. A fixed monthly care plan costs a little more than nothing, but updates, backups and small jobs are simply handled, and you always know exactly who to call. For most owners we speak to, that predictability is worth far more than shaving off a few pounds.

## How to budget without overpaying

Start with what your site does for you. If it brings in even one or two customers a month, a plan in the £27–£150 range is a rounding error against that value — and skipping it is a false economy. Set aside a fixed monthly figure so maintenance never has to compete with your other costs, and keep a small annual buffer, say £100–£300, for the occasional new page or seasonal refresh.
