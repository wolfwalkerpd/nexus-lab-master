Your website is often the first real impression someone gets of your business — long before they ring you, walk through the door or hand over any money. So it has a job to do, and doing it badly is expensive. This guide walks through the 10 things every small business website needs, in plain English, so you can open your own site tonight and spot exactly what is missing.

The good news is that you do not need to be technical to fix most of it. Whether you run a café in Leeds, a plumbing round in Bristol or a two-person accountancy practice in Surrey, the same short list of essentials decides whether a visitor turns into a customer or quietly clicks away to a competitor. It is the same list whether you have ten customers a week or a hundred.

## The 10 things every small business website needs

Here is the full checklist. Read it with your own site open in another tab. If you are missing more than two or three of these, that is usually the reason the phone is not ringing as often as it should — and most of them are quicker to fix than you think.

- A clear headline that says exactly what you do and who you do it for
- An obvious way to get in touch on every single page
- A phone number that taps straight to a call on mobile
- Fast loading, especially over a patchy phone signal
- Proof you can be trusted — reviews, real photos and credentials
- Your services explained plainly, with honest pricing signals
- A Google Business Profile linked to your website
- Local SEO so nearby customers can actually find you
- A mobile-first layout that works with one thumb
- Security, backups and someone keeping it updated

## Make it effortless to get in touch

The single most common mistake we see is hiding the contact details. Put your phone number in the header of every page, make it **tap-to-call** on mobile, and add a short contact form that asks for the bare minimum: a name, a number, and a line about what they need. Bury it, and people will simply ring someone else.

Add your address and opening hours as well. It sounds obvious, but it tells a nervous first-time visitor that you are a real, local business rather than a faceless website — and that reassurance is often what tips them into making contact.

> Most people decide within seconds whether to stay — so never make them dig for your phone number.

## Earn trust before the first phone call

People buy from businesses they believe in, and your website has to earn that belief before anyone calls. Real photos of your team and your finished work beat stock images every time. Add genuine reviews, any trade accreditations or professional memberships, and a straightforward page that says who you are and why you do this. Nobody expects a small business to look like a national brand, but they do expect it to look cared for.

Then lay your services out clearly and be honest about pricing. Even a 'from' figure or a typical range helps people self-qualify before they get in touch. When your [web design](/services/website-design) does the reassuring for you, the enquiries that land are warmer, better matched and far easier to win.

## Help local customers find you

Most local searches happen on a phone, and a huge share of them include the words **near me** or a town name. To show up for those, claim and fully complete your Google Business Profile, link it to your site, and make sure your pages actually name the towns, neighbourhoods and areas you serve.

Strong [local SEO](/services/seo) is the difference between being found by someone who is ready to buy today and being invisible while the business down the road takes the call. It builds up over months rather than overnight, so the sooner you start, the more you will have banked when it matters.

## Keep it fast, safe and up to date

A slow website loses visitors before it has even finished loading. Compress your images, keep the design light, and test it on a real phone over mobile data rather than your office wi-fi. Speed is not a nice-to-have; it quietly decides how many people stick around.

You also need the basics of housekeeping: HTTPS so browsers show the padlock, regular backups, and someone keeping software and plugins patched. An out-of-date site is one of the most common ways small businesses get hacked, so if your site was built years ago and nobody has touched it since, assume this is where the risk is hiding.
