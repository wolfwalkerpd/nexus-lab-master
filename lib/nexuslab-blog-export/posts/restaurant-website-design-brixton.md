## Put your menu where people can actually read it

The single most-visited page on any restaurant website is **the menu**. Yet plenty of Brixton spots still hide theirs in a PDF that opens sideways, or only ever post it as a photo on Instagram. If a customer struggles to read your menu, they quietly assume the worst about the food. Your menu should be real text on the page — quick to load, easy to scan, and simple for you to change when the specials do. It matters for search too: Google can read words on a page, but it cannot read the words trapped inside an image.

- Prices, dietary labels and allergens shown clearly, not buried in tiny print
- Sections a hungry customer can scan in seconds — brunch, mains, sides, drinks
- Easy for you to edit yourself when a dish sells out or the price of coffee shifts
- Photos that look like your actual plates, not stock images of someone else's food

## Make booking a table the easy bit

Brixton runs on its night-time economy. The O2 Academy alone pulls thousands of people into SW9 on a gig night, and plenty of them want dinner before doors open. If those people land on your site and cannot book in a couple of taps, you have handed the table to the place next door. Give them clear options: a booking widget, a big click-to-call button, WhatsApp for quick questions, and an honest note about walk-ins and larger groups. The same goes for the Sunday brunch queue — let people join a waitlist from their phone instead of drifting off into the market.

> A booking that takes two taps on a phone is worth more than the prettiest homepage you will ever build.
