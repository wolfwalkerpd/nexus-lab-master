Deciding between one-off vs monthly website payment plans is one of the first choices you'll face when you commission a new site, and it isn't as simple as picking the smaller number. Both models can be sensible. Both can also quietly cost you far more than you expected, depending on what's bundled in and, crucially, who owns the site at the end.

This guide breaks down what each model really includes, the true cost over three to five years, and the questions to ask before you sign anything. No jargon, no sales spin, just what we'd tell a friend running a shop, a salon or a [trades business](/industries) here in the UK.

## One-off vs monthly website payment plans, explained

Strip away the marketing and there are two ways to pay for a website. With a one-off payment, you agree a price, the site gets built, and it belongs to you. With a monthly payment plan, you pay little or nothing upfront and a set fee every month instead, sometimes for a fixed term, sometimes for as long as the site stays live.

Neither is a scam and neither is automatically better. What matters is what sits inside the number: the design and build, hosting, a domain, updates, support, and the part people forget to check, whether you own the finished site or are effectively renting it.

That doesn't make monthly wrong. Spreading cost has genuine value when money is tight. It just means the cheap-looking option is often the expensive one once you add up the years.

## Who actually owns the website?

This is the question that catches people out. With many monthly plans, you're leasing the site. Stop paying and it can be switched off, taking your pages, your content and sometimes your domain and email with it. You've spent thousands and have nothing to move elsewhere.

We take the opposite view: **you should own your website, your domain and your data**, full stop. No lock-in, no hostage situation if you ever decide to work with someone else. If a provider won't confirm in writing that the site is yours to keep, treat that as your answer. It's also worth checking how the [web design](/services/website-design) is scoped, because a cheap monthly template is a very different thing from a site built around your business.
