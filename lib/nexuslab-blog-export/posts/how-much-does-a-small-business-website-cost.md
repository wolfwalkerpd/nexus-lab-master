If you have ever asked how much a small business website costs, you already know the answer most agencies give: it depends. That is true, but it is not much help when you are trying to set a budget. So let us be more useful. In the UK, a professional small business website usually lands somewhere between a few hundred and a few thousand pounds, and the final figure comes down to a handful of things you can actually control.

Price on its own tells you very little, though. A £300 site and a £3,000 site can look almost identical in a screenshot and behave completely differently once real customers start using them. This guide breaks down the real ranges, explains what pushes the price up or down, and helps you spend on the things that actually bring in enquiries.

## The short answer: typical UK website costs

Here are the ranges we see most often for UK small businesses. Treat them as a rough map rather than a quote, because no two projects are quite the same.

Most local businesses, a plumber, a dental practice, a café or a firm of accountants, sit comfortably in the middle bands. You rarely need the most expensive option to look credible and win work.

- **DIY website builders** (Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy): around £0 to £30 a month, plus a lot of your own time. Fine for testing an idea, limiting once you want to grow.
- **Freelancer or template site**: roughly £300 to £1,500 for a small brochure website, depending on how much is bespoke.
- **Small studio or agency**: around £1,000 to £5,000 for a properly built site with real copywriting, SEO groundwork and someone to call when you need help.
- **Larger agency or complex build**: £5,000 and up, usually for e-commerce, booking systems or bigger content sites.

## What actually drives the price

When a quote seems high, or suspiciously low, it usually comes down to these factors. Understanding them helps you compare like for like.

- **Number of pages** and how much is custom versus dropped into a template.
- **Copywriting**: whether you supply the words or the studio writes them for you.
- **Design**: an off-the-shelf theme costs less than a design built around your brand.
- **Features**: a simple contact form is cheap; online booking, payments or customer logins add time.
- **SEO groundwork**: proper page structure, speed and local setup so you can actually be found.
- **Support afterwards**: hosting, updates and someone to fix things when they break.

> Price tells you what you paid. It does not tell you what you got.

## Why the cheapest option often costs the most

A bargain website is only a bargain if it does its job. The most common regret we hear is from owners who paid a little, got a site that looked fine but never brought in enquiries, then paid again to have it rebuilt within a year or two. Cheap sites often skip the unglamorous work that actually matters: fast loading, clear structure, mobile layout and local SEO. Most local searches now happen on a phone, so a site that is awkward on mobile quietly loses you customers every week.

There is also the cost of your time. DIY builders are genuinely capable, but the hours you spend wrestling with layouts and settings are hours you are not running your business.

## How to spend wisely, not just less

The goal is not the cheapest website or the most expensive one. It is the site that pays for itself by turning visitors into enquiries. Before you commit, it helps to be clear on what you want the site to do and how you will know it is working.

Get those four things straight and the price question becomes far easier to answer, because you know exactly what you are buying.

- Decide on the **one action** you want most visitors to take, whether that is a call, a booking or a form.
- Ask any provider to show you **live examples** of businesses like yours.
- Check whether **ongoing SEO** is included or extra, and get it in writing. Our [local SEO](/services/seo) work, for instance, is quoted per project.
- Confirm **who owns what** and what happens if you ever move on.
