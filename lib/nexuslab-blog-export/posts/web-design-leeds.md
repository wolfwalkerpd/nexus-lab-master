If you run a business in Leeds — a dental practice in Chapel Allerton, a trade covering the LS postcodes, a bar off Call Lane — your website has one job: turn a curious local into an enquiry. Good web design in Leeds isn't about winning design awards. It's about being the business someone picks when they're stood on Briggate, phone in hand, comparing you against three competitors.

Most of that comparing happens on a mobile, in a hurry, and often with 'near me' attached. A site that loads slowly, buries your phone number or reads like a printed brochure quietly costs you work every single week. This is how to get one built that actually pulls its weight — and what to watch for along the way.

## What Leeds customers expect before they get in touch

Leeds is a big, crowded market. You're competing for attention alongside the financial and legal firms around Wellington Place and Park Row, the independents lining Headingley and Chapel Allerton, and national chains with deep pockets. Add tens of thousands of students from two universities and a commuter belt stretching out to Horsforth, Otley and Wetherby, and you've got customers who scan fast and decide faster.

People here don't really read websites — they scan them for reasons to trust you and reasons to leave. Your first screen has a few seconds to show what you do, where you do it, and how to reach you. Get that right and the phone rings. Get it wrong and they're already back on Google looking at the next result.

## What good web design in Leeds gets right

A website that brings in enquiries isn't complicated — it's just deliberate. Most of the sites we tear down are missing the same handful of basics that separate a site that works from one that simply sits there:

- A clear headline that says what you do and where — 'Emergency electrician covering LS1 to LS28', not 'Welcome to our website'.
- Your phone number and a booking button on every screen, and easy to tap on a mobile.
- Proof near the top: genuine reviews, the areas you cover, any trade bodies you belong to.
- Fast pages — a shopper on 4G near the Corn Exchange won't wait five seconds for your images to load.
- One obvious next step on every page, so nobody has to hunt for how to reach you.
- Local pages naming the neighbourhoods you serve, so you appear for 'near me' searches.
