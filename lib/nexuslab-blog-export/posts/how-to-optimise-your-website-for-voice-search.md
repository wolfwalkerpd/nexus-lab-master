More and more people are talking to their phones, watches and smart speakers instead of typing. 'Hey Siri, where's the nearest dentist open on a Saturday?' or 'OK Google, find me a plumber near me.' If your website only answers the questions people type into a search box, you're quietly missing all the ones they say out loud. The good news is that learning how to optimise your website for voice search isn't some dark art. It comes down to one simple idea: sounding like a real person giving a clear, quick answer to a real question.

This is a plain-English guide for busy small-business owners across the UK. There's no code to write and no jargon to wade through, just the handful of things that genuinely make a difference.

## What voice search actually is

Voice search is simply anyone using their voice, rather than their thumbs, to find something online. That covers phones, smart speakers on the kitchen counter, car dashboards and even the search bar built into most laptops now. Over the last few years it has quietly become part of everyday life, especially for quick questions asked on the move.

The important thing to grasp is that spoken searches behave differently from typed ones. When you type, you clip your words down to the bare bones: 'dentist manchester saturday'. When you speak, you use a full, natural sentence: 'which dentists in Manchester are open on a Saturday?' Voice queries tend to be longer, more conversational and phrased as proper questions. The vast majority happen on a mobile, and a large share are local, someone hunting for a business near them, right now.

## How to optimise your website for voice search

There's no single button to press. Optimising for voice is really a set of small, sensible improvements, and the happy side effect is that they make your site better for every visitor, not just the ones talking to their phone. Here's where to put your effort.

- Write in natural, conversational language that matches how people actually speak, not stiff marketing-speak.
- Target real question phrases, like 'how much does a new boiler cost?' rather than just 'boiler prices'.
- Give a clear, direct answer early on the page, because assistants read out short, tidy responses.
- Add a genuinely useful FAQ section that mirrors the questions customers ask out loud.
- Make the site fast and comfortable to use on a phone, often on a patchy signal.
- Keep your local details accurate and identical everywhere they appear online.

## Answer real questions, the way people ask them

Sit down for ten minutes and list every question a customer asks you before they buy. 'Do you offer free quotes?' 'How long does a bathroom fit take?' 'Do you cover my postcode?' Those exact phrases are what people speak into their phones, word for word. Put the questions on your site as clear headings and answer each one in a sentence or two, plainly and honestly.

This is where a well-built FAQ really earns its keep. It matches conversational queries almost perfectly, and it gives search engines a clean, self-contained answer to read out. Strong [SEO](/services/seo) has never been about tricking Google; it's about being the clearest, most helpful answer on the page, and voice search rewards that more than ever.

> Voice assistants don't read out your whole page. They read the single clearest answer they can find, so make sure that answer is yours.

## Local voice search: showing up for 'near me'

A huge slice of voice searches are both local and urgent. Someone standing on the high street asking their phone 'who's the closest florist?' is ready to walk in or pick up the phone within minutes. To win those moments, your local details have to be spot on and easy for a machine to read.

Claim and fully complete your Google Business Profile, then make sure your business name, address and phone number match exactly across your website and every online listing. Add your opening hours, the areas you cover and a few honest photos. When someone asks for a business 'near me' or 'open now', that's the information their assistant leans on to decide who to recommend, so treat it as seriously as your shopfront.

## Common voice-search mistakes to avoid

A few habits quietly hold small-business sites back. Steer clear of these and you'll be in good shape.

None of this is complicated, but it's easy to let slip when you're busy running the business. If you want a second pair of eyes, we're happy to [take a proper look at your site](/contact) and point out the quick wins.

- Stuffing pages with keywords instead of writing like a human, which reads badly and helps no one.
- Burying your answer three paragraphs down, where an assistant is unlikely to reach it.
- Letting your address or opening hours drift out of date across different listings.
- Ignoring page speed and mobile layout because the site 'looks fine' on your desktop.
