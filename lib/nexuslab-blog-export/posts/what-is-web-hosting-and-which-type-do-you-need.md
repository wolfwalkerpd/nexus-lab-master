If you've ever tried to get a website off the ground, one question tends to stop you in your tracks: what is web hosting and which type do you need? It sounds technical, but the idea behind it is genuinely simple. Web hosting is the space you rent on a powerful computer, called a server, that keeps your website switched on around the clock — so anyone who types in your address sees your business rather than an error message.

The confusing bit isn't what hosting does. It's the menu you get handed: shared, VPS, cloud, dedicated, managed. Providers often make it sound more complicated than it is, and small businesses end up paying for power they will never use. For most UK firms — a plumber in Leeds, a café off a Bristol high street, a two-person accountancy practice — the right choice is usually simpler and cheaper than the sales page suggests. Let's clear it up.

## Think of hosting as renting premises online

The easiest way to picture web hosting is to compare it to a shop. Your domain name (something like yourbusiness.co.uk) is the address people use to find you. Your website is the shop itself — the shelves, the signage, the products. Hosting is the physical premises that everything sits inside. Rent that space, and your shop is open to the public. Stop paying for it, and the doors close.

A host gives you a few practical things: storage for your pages, text and images; bandwidth, which is the amount of visitor traffic you can handle; and uptime, the promise that your server stays online. Good hosting is quiet and invisible — pages load quickly, nothing falls over when you get busy, and you rarely think about it. Bad hosting shows up as slow pages and downtime, both of which cost you customers and, over time, hurt your [search rankings](/services/seo).

## The main types of web hosting explained

Almost every plan you will see is a version of one of these five. Here is what each one really means, in plain terms.

- **Shared hosting** — Your site sits on one server alongside many others, sharing the cost and the resources. It's the cheapest option and perfectly good for a standard small-business or brochure site. The trade-off is that a very busy neighbour can occasionally slow things down.
- **VPS (virtual private server)** — Still one physical server, but partitioned so you get a guaranteed slice of power that's yours alone. A sensible step up if your site grows busy or runs a small online shop.
- **Dedicated hosting** — A whole server to yourself. Powerful, expensive, and almost always overkill for a local business. This is for large sites with heavy traffic.
- **Cloud hosting** — Your site runs across a network of servers, so it can scale up when traffic spikes and you pay for what you use. Flexible and reliable, though the pricing can be less predictable.
- **Managed WordPress hosting** — Hosting tuned specifically for WordPress, with updates, security and backups handled for you. Costs a little more, but saves you the technical upkeep.

## Which type of web hosting do you need?

Here is the honest answer most small businesses never hear: you almost certainly need shared or managed hosting, not the pricey tiers. If you're running a website that shows what you do, builds trust and brings in enquiries — the kind of site most tradespeople, clinics, shops and professional firms need — shared hosting comfortably handles it. Choose managed hosting instead if your site is built on WordPress and you would rather not touch the technical side.

You only need to think about VPS or cloud hosting once you're doing serious volume: a busy online shop, thousands of daily visitors, or an app-like site. And dedicated hosting? Unless you're a large organisation with an IT budget to match, you can safely ignore it.

## Hosting, domain and email are three different things

This trips people up constantly, so it's worth stating plainly. Your domain is your address, and you rent it separately, usually for £10 to £20 a year. Your hosting is the space your website lives in. And business email — like hello@yourbusiness.co.uk — is a separate service again, though it's often bought from the same provider. Keeping them clear matters, because you want to own all three yourself rather than have them locked inside someone else's account. If you ever change designers, you should be able to take your website, domain and data with you. That's the whole point of ownership, and it's how we build every site.

## What to look for in a host

If you do decide to sort hosting yourself, a few things separate a decent host from a frustrating one. Run down this quick checklist before you buy.

- **UK or nearby servers** — physically closer servers mean faster load times for your UK visitors.
- **A free SSL certificate** — the padlock in the browser bar; standard now, and something Google expects.
- **Daily backups** — so a mistake or a hack doesn't wipe out months of work.
- **Real support** — a human you can reach when something breaks, ideally by phone.
- **Room to grow** — an easy path to upgrade if your traffic climbs, without rebuilding everything.
