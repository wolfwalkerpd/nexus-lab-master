If you have looked into advertising your business online, someone has probably told you to build a landing page. It gets mentioned a lot and explained rarely. So this guide answers it plainly: what is a landing page, and when do you need one? No jargon, no marketing waffle — just what it is and whether it is worth your money.

The short version is that a landing page is not your homepage, and it is not your whole website. It is a single page with one job. Once that idea clicks, deciding whether you need one gets a lot easier — and below we walk through the exact moments when a landing page pays for itself.

## What a landing page actually is

A landing page is a single web page built around one goal. That goal is usually a specific action you want the visitor to take: book an appointment, request a quote, call you, or buy one thing. The name is literal — it is the page people land on after clicking a link, whether that link sits in a Google advert, an email, an Instagram post, or a search result.

Say you run a boiler-repair business in Leeds and you are paying for Google Ads on the term emergency boiler repair. The person clicking is not in the mood to browse. They want a phone number and the reassurance that you can help today. A good landing page gives them exactly that and nothing else — no menu, no blog, no list of your fifteen other services. One message, one action.

## How a landing page differs from your homepage

Your homepage is a reception desk. Its job is to greet everyone and send them in the right direction — services here, about us there, contact over there. That is exactly what it should do, because homepage visitors arrive with all sorts of intentions. A landing page is the opposite. It is a corridor with one door at the end.

- **One goal, not many.** The whole page pushes towards a single action instead of offering ten routes.
- **Fewer distractions.** Landing pages often drop the main menu, so there is nowhere to wander off to.
- **A matched message.** The headline echoes the advert or link that brought the visitor in, so the page feels like the right place to be.
- **Easy to measure.** Because there is one action, you can see precisely how many people took it and what each enquiry cost you.

## When do you actually need a landing page?

Here is the honest answer: you need a landing page when you are sending a specific group of people to do a specific thing. Most often that means you are spending money to get them there. If any of the following sound like you, a landing page will almost certainly earn its keep.

- **You are running paid ads.** Sending paid clicks to your homepage is like paying for a taxi and getting out three streets early. A focused page converts far more of that traffic.
- **You are promoting one offer.** A seasonal deal, a new treatment, an open day — anything with a single, clear ask.
- **You are targeting one service or one area.** A page just for kitchen fitting in Bristol will beat a general services page for that search, whatever your [industry](/industries).
- **You are collecting leads.** A downloadable guide, a quote form, or a waiting list all work best on a page with no competing links.
- **You are testing a new idea.** Before you build out a whole new service, a landing page tells you whether anyone actually wants it.

> A landing page turns a click you paid for into a customer you can keep.

## What makes a landing page work

A landing page is simple, but simple is not the same as easy. The ones that perform tend to share the same handful of ingredients.

If you also want the page to pull in visitors from Google for free, not just from ads, it needs to be written and structured for search. That is where a landing page and [local SEO](/services/seo) start working together — the same focused page can rank for a specific service in a specific town while it converts your paid traffic.

- **A headline that matches the click.** If your advert said same-day boiler repair, the page should say it back within the first second.
- **One clear call to action, repeated.** Ask for the same thing near the top and again at the bottom. Do not make people hunt.
- **Proof that you are real.** Genuine reviews, photos of your actual work, and any accreditations that matter in your trade.
- **Fast loading on a phone.** Most local searches happen on a mobile, and a slow page loses people before they read a word.
- **Short and focused.** Cut anything that does not move the visitor towards that one action.

## The quick wins

- A landing page is a single page with one job: to get the visitor to take one specific action.
- Your homepage points people everywhere; a landing page points them at one thing.
- You need one most when you are paying for traffic — ads, a single promotion, or lead collection.
- Sometimes a stronger homepage beats a new landing page. Be honest about which you actually need.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the difference between a landing page and a website?

A website is a collection of pages that together tell your whole story — home, services, about, contact and so on. A landing page is a single page with one focused goal, usually reached from an advert or a link. You can have a landing page as part of a website, or as a standalone page built for one specific campaign.

### Do I need a landing page if I already have a website?

Not always. If your homepage already turns visitors into enquiries, you may not need one yet. But the moment you start paying for ads or pushing a single offer, a dedicated landing page will usually convert far better than sending that traffic to a busy homepage.

### Can I build a landing page myself?

You can, with tools like Wix or Squarespace, and for a quick test that is a fair place to start. The catch is that the details that make a page convert — a matched headline, a fast mobile load, one obvious action — are easy to get wrong. If the page is spending your ad budget, it is usually worth getting it built properly.
