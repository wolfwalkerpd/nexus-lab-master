Hiring a web designer can feel like a big leap, especially if it is your first time. The reassuring truth is that the result usually comes down to preparation. Knowing what to prepare before hiring a web designer means you get a better website, sooner, and often for less money — because the person building it is never left waiting on you.

You do not need to be technical. You just need to turn up with a clear idea of what you want, the raw materials to build it, and a few sensible questions. Here is exactly what to sort out first.

## Start with what you want the website to do

Before anyone touches a design, get clear on the job you want the site to do. A plumber chasing emergency call-outs needs a phone number that is impossible to miss and a page for every area they cover. A village cafe wants table bookings and a menu people can read on their phone over breakfast. A solicitor wants to look trustworthy and make it easy to request a call back.

Write down one or two main goals, a sentence each. Everything — the layout, the wording, the buttons — should push towards those goals. If you cannot say what the site is for, the design will drift, and you will feel it when the quotes come in.

## Gather your content before you start

Content is what slows most projects down. Designers can build fast, but they cannot invent your opening hours or photograph your work for you. Pull the following into one folder before you brief anyone.

If some of this is missing, do not stall the whole project. Good studios fold copywriting and photography guidance into the [web design and build](/services/website-design) itself, so you can hand over rough notes and let them shape it.

- Your logo, ideally the original file rather than a screenshot, plus any brand colours you already use.
- Real photos of your premises, team or finished work — a phone camera is fine if the light is good.
- A plain list of your services or products, with prices if you choose to show them.
- Contact details, opening hours and the areas you cover.
- Any reviews or testimonials you are happy to display, with the customer's first name.
- Whatever wording you already have — an old site, a leaflet, even notes in an email.

## Know your budget and rough timeline

You will get a straighter answer from any designer if you can say what you are willing to spend. You do not need a precise figure, just a bracket. It also helps to decide when you need the site live — before a busy season, a new location opening, or a funding deadline.

Prices vary, but fixed-price studios make this easier because you know the cost up front. ## List the features you actually need

It is easy to ask for everything and pay for things you will never use. Separate what you genuinely need now from what would be nice one day.

Every build should include a simple admin panel so you can update text and images yourself without ringing anyone. Ask about that before you sign.

- A contact form and a click-to-call button that works on mobile.
- Online booking or appointments, if customers expect to reserve a slot.
- A simple shop, if you sell products directly.
- A gallery or portfolio to show off your work.
- A blog or news area — useful if being found on Google matters. If it does, plan for [local SEO](/services/seo) early rather than bolting it on later.

## The quick wins

- Decide the one or two jobs your website must do before you brief anyone.
- Gather your logo, photos, services and reviews into a single folder to avoid delays.
- Know your budget bracket and go-live date so quotes come back fast and realistic.
- Ask every designer who owns the site, whether the price is fixed, and what support follows launch.

### How much does it cost to hire a web designer in the UK?

It varies widely, which is why fixed pricing helps you plan. Our websites are fixed-price from £477 for a Starter site, £1,170 for Growth and £2,370 for Complete, with care plans from £27 a month. SEO and redesigns are quoted per project, so you always know the cost before you commit.

### How long does it take to build a website?

Most of our sites go live in three to six weeks. The single biggest factor is how quickly your content — photos, wording, services and opening hours — is ready, so the preparation in this guide directly shortens the timeline.

### Do I have to write all the content myself?

No. It helps enormously to gather the raw material — rough notes, a services list, photos and any old copy — but you do not need to be a writer. Copywriting can be part of the build, so you can hand over the basics and let us shape them.

### What do I actually need to give a web designer to get started?

At a minimum: a clear goal for the site, your logo and brand colours, some real photos, a list of services or products, your contact details and opening hours, and any reviews you are happy to show. That is enough to begin a strong first draft.

### Do I own the website after it is built?

With us, yes. You own your website, domain and data outright, with no lock-in. Always ask this of any designer before you sign, because some arrangements leave you unable to move your site elsewhere later.
