You've got a website, you've got a contact form, and yet the enquiries just aren't coming. It's one of the most common frustrations we hear from small-business owners across the UK. The good news is that learning how to set up a contact form that actually gets leads has very little to do with code and everything to do with a few practical decisions. Get those right and the same visitors you already have will start reaching out.

## Why most contact forms quietly lose leads

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a contact form can look perfectly fine and still be costing you business every single week. The problem is rarely the form itself — it's the small points of friction that pile up. A form buried at the bottom of the page. Too many nosy questions. No reassurance about what happens next. A broken submit button nobody has tested in months.

Most local searches happen on a phone, often while someone is standing in a queue or half-watching the telly. If your form is fiddly on mobile, asks for a person's life story, or leaves them wondering whether the message even sent, they simply close the tab. You never find out they were interested. That's the real cost — not the enquiries you lose, but the ones you never knew you had.

## Ask for less: the fields that earn you more replies

Every extra field you add is another reason to give up. As a rule, ask only for what you genuinely need to start a conversation — you can gather the rest once someone has replied. For most small businesses, that means keeping it to the essentials:

Skip anything you can look up or ask later. Job titles, full postal addresses and 'how did you hear about us?' can all wait. Fewer fields feels less like a form and more like firing off a quick message — and that's exactly the feeling you want.

- Name — a first name is usually enough to feel personal
- Email or phone — let people choose whichever they prefer
- A short message box — one open question beats five tick-boxes
- One optional detail that helps you quote, like 'what do you need?' or a rough budget

> Every extra field is another reason for a busy person to give up and close the tab.

## Put the form where people are ready to act

A form only works if people can find it the moment they decide to get in touch. Don't tuck it away on a single contact page and hope for the best. The strongest sites give people an obvious next step wherever interest is likely to peak — a short form in the footer of every page, a clear button after you've described a service, and a proper contact page for those who go looking. Good [website design](/services/website-design) treats the form as part of the journey, not an afterthought bolted on at the end. On mobile especially, put a tappable phone number right beside the form — some people will always prefer to call.

## Tell people what happens next

Sending a message into the void is uncomfortable. A few words of reassurance beside your form remove that hesitation. Tell people when they'll hear back — 'we usually reply the same working day' — and show a genuine thank-you message once they hit send, rather than leaving them staring at the same empty form wondering if anything happened.

Then the part that matters most: reply quickly. Someone who fills in a form is warm right now, not next Tuesday. The business that wins the work is very often just the one that answers first. If enquiries tend to land while you're on the tools or with a customer, set up an instant email alert so nothing slips through the cracks.

## Test it like a real customer

You'd be surprised how many forms quietly break — a plugin update, a changed email address, a spam filter swallowing every message. If you haven't tested yours this month, assume it might be broken and check:

- Fill it in from your own phone and confirm the message actually arrives
- Look in spam and junk, then whitelist your form's sending address
- Add a simple spam guard so real enquiries aren't lost among junk

## The quick wins

- Ask for the fewest fields possible — a name, one contact detail and a short message is usually enough.
- Put a clear form or call button wherever interest peaks, not just on a hidden contact page.
- Reassure people what happens next, then reply fast — the business that answers first usually wins the work.
- Test your form every month, because broken forms fail silently.

## Frequently asked questions

### What's the biggest mistake people make with contact forms?

Asking for too much. Every extra field lowers the number of people who finish. Start with a name, one way to reach them and a short message, then gather the detail once they've replied.

### How many fields should a contact form have?

For most local businesses, three or four is plenty. Aim to make it feel like sending a quick message rather than filling in paperwork — the shorter it looks, the more people complete it.
