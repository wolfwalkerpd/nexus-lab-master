Keyword research basics for local businesses come down to one simple idea: find the exact words people type when they want what you sell, then make sure your website speaks their language. You don't need a marketing degree or costly software, just a bit of curiosity about your own customers and an afternoon to write things down.

Get this right and the rest of your marketing gets easier, because every page has a clear job to do. Get it wrong and you end up chasing words nobody searches, or fighting national brands for terms you will never win. Here is how to do the groundwork properly, without the jargon.

## What keyword research actually is

Keyword research is simply working out which phrases your potential customers use in Google, how often they use them, and how much competition there is for each one. A keyword rarely means a single word. The useful ones are short phrases like 'emergency plumber Leeds' or 'dog groomer near me'.

For a local business, the goal is not to chase the biggest numbers. A plumber in Sheffield does not need to rank for 'plumber' across the whole country; they need the handful of people in Sheffield with a leaking pipe right now. Those smaller, more specific searches are worth far more to you, because the people making them are close by and ready to book. That focus on nearby, ready-to-act searches is the heart of good [local SEO](/services/seo).

## Keyword research basics for local businesses, step by step

Here is a simple sequence you can work through in a single sitting:

None of this needs special training. You are already the expert on your own customers. This just turns what you know into a plan you can act on.

- **Brainstorm your services.** List everything you offer in plain words, the way a customer would say it rather than the way it reads on your invoice. 'Boiler repair', not 'domestic heating remediation'.
- **Add your locations.** Pair each service with the towns, neighbourhoods and postcodes you actually cover: 'boiler repair Wandsworth', 'boiler repair SW18'.
- **Think about intent.** Note whether someone using that phrase wants to buy now, compare prices, or just learn something. The ready-to-buy searches matter most.
- **Check the real numbers.** Use a free tool to see roughly how often each phrase is searched and how tough the competition looks.
- **Pick your winners.** Choose a shortlist that is specific enough to win but popular enough to be worth the effort.

## The searches worth chasing

Not every search is equal. The ones that turn into enquiries usually share three traits: they are specific, they are local, and they carry clear intent. 'Cheap haircut' is vague and could mean almost anything. 'Balayage salon Didsbury' tells you exactly who is searching and what they want.

These longer, more specific phrases are often called long-tail keywords. Each gets fewer searches on its own, but there are thousands of them, the competition is gentler, and the people using them are much closer to picking up the phone. Across the [industries we work with](/industries), from salons to trades, that pattern holds: the narrow searches quietly bring in the best work.

> The best local keyword is not the one with the most searches. It is the one your ideal customer types the moment they are ready to book.

## Free tools you can try this afternoon

You do not need a paid subscription to begin. A few free tools will get you most of the way, and an hour with a notepad will hand you more usable keywords than most businesses ever act on:

- **Google autocomplete.** Start typing a service into the search bar and note the suggestions. Those are real phrases real people use.
- **'People also ask' and 'Related searches'.** Scroll any results page and you will find dozens of question-style keywords for free.
- **Google Keyword Planner.** Free with a Google Ads account, it shows rough search volumes and related terms.
- **Google Business Profile insights.** Once you have claimed your listing, it reveals the search terms already bringing people to you.

## Turning keywords into pages that rank

Research only pays off when it shapes your website. Once you have a shortlist, give each important phrase a home. Your main service pages should target your biggest services, and a separate, genuinely written page for each town or service you cover helps you appear in more local searches. Copying a page and swapping the town name does the opposite, because Google treats near-identical pages as spam.

Weave your chosen phrases naturally into page titles, headings and opening paragraphs, then write for humans first. ## Frequently asked questions

### What is keyword research, in plain English?

It is the process of finding the words and phrases people type into Google when they are looking for a business like yours, then checking how often each is searched and how much competition it has. For a local business, the aim is to spot the specific, ready-to-buy searches, like 'emergency electrician Bristol', and make sure your website is built around them.

### Do I need paid tools to do keyword research?

No. You can get a long way with Google's autocomplete, the 'People also ask' and 'Related searches' boxes, the free Google Keyword Planner, and the insights inside your Google Business Profile. Paid tools add detail and save time, but the basics cost nothing but an afternoon.

### How many keywords should a local business target?

Start small. A handful of strong, specific phrases tied to your main services and locations will do more than a long list of vague ones. As you add well-written pages for each service and area you cover, you will naturally rank for hundreds of related searches over time, so there is no need to force it.
