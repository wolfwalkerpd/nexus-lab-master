Most small-business websites are polite, tidy and completely forgettable. They describe the business, list a few services, and then sit there hoping someone gets in touch. If yours feels a bit like that, you are not doing anything wrong on purpose. Nobody teaches a plumber, a dentist or a cafe owner how to write website content that converts a browser into a booking.

The good news is that this is a skill, not a talent. You do not need clever slogans or a marketing degree. You need to say the right things, in the right order, in language your customers actually use. Below is the exact approach we use when we write for clients, laid out so you can do it yourself, or at least know what good looks like.

## Start with the customer, not your business

Here is the mistake almost everyone makes. They open with a line about themselves: "Established in 2009, we are a family-run firm committed to quality and customer service." Nice, but your visitor does not care yet. They arrived with a problem, and in the first few seconds they are asking one silent question: "Can this business fix my thing?"

So answer it. If you are a boiler engineer in Leeds, your homepage should say something like "No hot water or heating in Leeds? We usually get you a same-week visit." That single line does more work than three paragraphs about your heritage. Write down the actual words your customers use when they describe their problem, then put those words near the top of the page.

- Lead with the problem you solve, not the year you were founded
- Use the customer's language, not industry terms ("blocked drain", not "drainage solutions")
- Make the first sentence answer "am I in the right place?"
- Save the company history for lower down, or the About page

## Write for one person reading on a phone

Most local searches happen on a mobile, often while someone is standing in a cold kitchen or sitting in a waiting room. That changes how you should write. Long, dense paragraphs that look fine on a laptop become an intimidating wall of grey on a small screen.

Picture one real customer and write as if you are speaking to them across a counter. Short sentences. Plenty of white space. One idea per paragraph. Read it back out loud, and if you run out of breath before the full stop, cut it in half. Warm, plain English beats clever every single time.

> If you run out of breath before the full stop, the sentence is too long.

## How to write website content that converts: the four-part structure

Every page that converts tends to follow the same simple shape. You do not need to be fancy, you just need all four parts present and in order. Missing one is usually why a page feels flat.

Work through these in sequence for your homepage and each main service page. It keeps you honest and stops you drifting into features nobody asked about. If you want to see how this looks applied across a full build, our approach to [website design](/services/website-design) is built around exactly this structure.

- **The promise**: what you do and who for, in one clear line at the top
- **The proof**: why you can be trusted (reviews, guarantees, years of experience, real photos)
- **The path**: what happens next, step by step, so there are no surprises
- **The prompt**: a clear, single next action, repeated where it makes sense

## Turn features into benefits your customer feels

A feature is a fact about what you do. A benefit is what that fact means for the customer's life. People buy the benefit, so your job is to translate. "Fixed-price quotes" is a feature. "You know the full cost before we start, so there is no nasty surprise on the invoice" is the benefit, and it is far more persuasive.

Go through your services and, next to each feature, write "which means..." and finish the sentence. A dentist offering "same-day emergency appointments" really means "you will not have to sit at home in pain waiting for next week." That is the version that gets the phone to ring. When you are ready to talk pricing, be just as plain: our own [pricing](/pricing) is fixed and published for the same reason, because vague costs make people hesitate.

## Tell them exactly what to do next

A page can be perfectly written and still fail if it forgets to ask for the sale. Do not assume the visitor will figure out the next step on their own. Tell them plainly what to do and what will happen when they do it, so there is no risk in taking action.

Keep it to one main call to action per page and repeat it where it naturally fits, rather than scattering five different links that pull people in different directions. "Get in touch" is weak because it asks the customer to do the thinking. "Send us a photo of the leak and we will tell you roughly what it will cost" is strong because it lowers the effort and removes the mystery. ## Edit ruthlessly, then check it against reality

First drafts are always too long and too polite. Once you have written a page, go back and cut anything that does not help the reader decide. Delete the throat-clearing intros, the "we pride ourselves on" filler, and any sentence you could not say out loud to a customer without cringing.

Then do one final test. Give the page to someone who knows nothing about your business and ask them three questions: what does this business do, why would I choose them, and what do I do next? If they can answer all three in a few seconds, your content is doing its job. If they hesitate, you have found exactly what to fix.

## The quick wins

- Every feature should be followed by "which means..." and a real human benefit
- Specific beats vague: numbers, timescales and plain outcomes build trust
- One clear next step converts better than five competing options

## Frequently asked questions

### How long should a page of website content be?

Long enough to answer the customer's questions and no longer. For a local service page, that is often 300 to 600 words. Focus on covering the promise, the proof, the process and a clear next step. A short page that is clear will always beat a long page that rambles.

### Do I need to worry about SEO keywords when writing?

Write for the human first, then check your keywords second. Use the natural phrases your customers actually search for, like "emergency electrician Bristol", in your headings and opening lines.
