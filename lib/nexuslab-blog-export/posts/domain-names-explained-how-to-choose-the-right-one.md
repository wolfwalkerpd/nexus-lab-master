Buying your first web address can feel oddly high-stakes. It's cheap, it takes five minutes, and yet it's the name your customers will type, say out loud and trust for years. So let's have domain names explained properly, and walk through how to choose the right one for your business without second-guessing yourself.

A domain name is simply your address on the web, the bit people type into the browser, like yourbakery.co.uk. You rent it, usually a year at a time, and while you can technically change it later, it's far better to get it right the first time. Here's how.

## Domain names explained, in plain English

Your domain name is your address on the web. When someone types yourbakery.co.uk into their browser, or Google shows it in the results, that's your domain. It has two parts: the name you choose (yourbakery) and the ending (.co.uk), sometimes called the extension.

You don't buy a domain outright the way you'd buy a van. You register it, usually for a year or two at a time, and renew it to keep it. As long as you keep renewing, it stays yours, and nobody else can take it.

## Start with the name people already know

The safest starting point is the name your customers already use for you. If your sign says Marlow Family Dental, then marlowfamilydental.co.uk is instantly recognisable and nobody has to guess. Matching your domain to your trading name keeps everything joined up across your van, your cards and your Google listing.

If the exact name is taken, don't panic. Adding your town or your trade often does the trick, and can even help locally: think marlowdental.co.uk or marlowfamilydentist.co.uk. What you want to avoid is drifting so far from your real name that people who have heard of you can't find you.

## Pick the right ending: .co.uk or .com?

For most UK local businesses, .co.uk is the natural choice. It tells people at a glance that you're British and that you serve the UK, which is exactly what a customer in Leeds or Cardiff wants to see. A .com tends to suit businesses with national or wider ambitions, or brands that want to look bigger than one town.

You'll also see newer endings like .uk, .london, or trade-specific ones like .dental. They can work, but tread carefully, as many customers still instinctively type .co.uk and may end up somewhere else. If you can, register both the .co.uk and the .com to protect your name, and point one at the other.

## The rules for a name that actually works

A good domain is one a customer can hear once and type correctly. Before you commit, run your shortlist past a few simple checks:

- Keep it short. Fewer letters means fewer typos and easier word-of-mouth.
- Make it easy to say out loud. If you have to spell it out twice on the phone, think again.
- Skip hyphens and numbers. Is it plumbers-24-7 or plumbers24seven? Nobody remembers, so drop them.
- Don't stuff in keywords. Something like cheap-emergency-plumber-london.co.uk looks like spam and puts people off.
- Check how it reads run together. Odd combinations appear when two innocent words collide, so read it as one string first.
- Test it on someone else. Say it aloud and ask them to write it down. If they get it right, you're onto a winner.

> The best domain names are boring in the best way: clear, short and impossible to get wrong.

## Buy it yourself, and make sure you own it

Here's the part people wish they'd known sooner. Whoever registers the domain, and holds the login, effectively controls it. Register it in your own name or your company's name, using an email address you'll keep, and store the login somewhere safe. ## The quick wins

- Match your domain to the business name people already know, and add your town or trade if it's taken.
- For most UK local businesses .co.uk is the safe pick; choose .com for national ambitions.
- Keep it short, easy to say, and free of hyphens and numbers.

## Frequently asked questions

### Should I choose .co.uk or .com for my business?

For most UK local businesses, .co.uk is the safe choice because it clearly signals you're British and serving UK customers. Pick .com if you have national or wider ambitions, and if you can, register both to protect your name.

### Can I change my domain name later?

Yes, but it's more hassle than it sounds. You'd need to redirect the old address, update your listings, print new cards and give search engines time to catch up. It's much easier to choose a name you're happy with from the start.
